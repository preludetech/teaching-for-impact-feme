## Why this matters

In the previous section, you designed learning activities that support participation and engagement.

This section focuses on how those activities lead to meaningful learning through practice, feedback, and iteration.

Learning does not happen through exposure alone. Participants need:

- opportunities to practise
- feedback to improve
- time to revise and strengthen their work

Without this, even well-designed activities remain theoretical and difficult to apply.

!!! quote "This section helps you move from..."
    *“Did I deliver the activity?”*  
    to:  
    *“Did learners practise, receive feedback, and improve within the activity?”*

## Core concepts

!!! abstract "Practice, feedback, and iteration"
    Learning becomes more robust when learners apply skills in realistic tasks, receive useful feedback, and improve through repeated cycles of attempt, response, and revision.

**Practice**  
Practice should align with outcomes, reflect real use, and include enough support to help learners make progress.

**Feedback**  
Useful feedback is specific, timely, and actionable. It helps learners better understand what is working and what to improve next.

**Iteration and productive challenge**  
Improvement typically happens through cycles of attempt, feedback, and revision. Learners do not need to get everything right on the first try. Productive challenge is part of learning when it is supported and followed by feedback.

## Practical guidance

### Step 1 — Design meaningful practice

Design activities so learners:

- apply key skills
- work on realistic tasks
- connect practice directly to outcomes

Use a mix of:

- guided practice
- independent practice
- collaborative practice

### Step 2 — Design feedback as part of the activity

Plan feedback as part of the activity:

- include checkpoints
- define what good performance looks like
- allow time for peer and facilitator feedback

### Step 3 — Make feedback useful

Useful feedback typically:

- focus on specific aspects of performance
- include next steps
- arrive while revision is still possible

### Step 4 — Build in iteration and versioning

Create opportunities for learners to revise and improve their work.

Simple approaches include:

- first attempt → feedback → revised version
- returning to a task in a later session
- using simple versions such as v0, v1, and v2 to make progress visible

### Step 5 — Enable peer learning

Support participants to learn from each other:

- use peer review
- encourage explanation of approaches
- make feedback respectful and constructive
- ensure participation remains inclusive

### Decision point

> How much opportunity will learners have to practise, receive feedback, and improve?

- Limited practice with minimal feedback
- Practice with some feedback but little revision
- Structured practice with feedback and multiple opportunities to improve

Your choice influences how well learning translates into real-world capability.

## Example

- **Context:** A team is designing training for researchers to analyse and communicate climate data.
- **Decision:** Should participants complete a single activity, or have opportunities to improve their work?
- **Action:** They design a sequence where participants complete an initial analysis, receive peer and facilitator feedback, and then revise their work. They label the versions v0, v1, and v2 so progress is visible. They also include a follow-up discussion on what changed and why.
- **Outcome:** Participants tend to produce stronger analyses, better understand their mistakes, and gain confidence in applying the method independently.
