Today's arc

You'll work through a decision workflow:

  1. What do your materials assume?
  2. What exists and does it fit?
  3. What are you allowed to do with it?
  4. What needs to change?
  5. What do you need to create?
  6. How do you make it usable for others?
~2 min. Frame this as a progression: "We move from analysis to action. The first half is about understanding what's possible. The second half is about doing the work." Then transition into openness framing.

Designing for openness

An open resource allows you to:

  • Retain — keep a copy
  • Reuse — use it in your teaching
  • Revise — modify or adapt it
  • Remix — combine it with other materials
  • Redistribute — share it with others

These are the 5 Rs of open educational resources.

~3 min. Keep this light — this is not a licensing lesson. The goal is to establish the mindset: openness is about what you can do with a resource, not just whether you can access it. "A resource you can view is not necessarily open. A resource becomes open when you have permission to act on it."

You're not starting from scratch

Designing for openness is not just about creating new materials.

It's about working with what already exists — reusing, adapting, and localising it for your context.

~2 min. Ground it in participants' reality: "Most of you are working with existing materials, partial materials, or just ideas. That's fine — this process works at any stage."

Three questions

Your Part 1 outcome determines:

  • What can I use?
  • What needs to change?
  • What do I need to create?
~2 min. Connect directly to Part 1: "Yesterday, you defined what you want learners to be able to do. That outcome now determines what you can reuse, what you must adapt, and what you may need to create." Transition: "We start by looking at what your materials expect people to already know, have, or be able to do."

Exercise 1: What do materials expect?

Individual → whole group

Consider the following instruction:

"Download the dataset and analyse it in Excel."

What does this expect someone to already know, have, or be able to do?

~3 min for this intro. Use the example to make the idea concrete. Take 2–3 quick answers from the room before moving to individual work. Expected responses: assumes Excel access, assumes data literacy, assumes English, assumes a specific dataset format.

Exercise 1: Individual work

5–7 min · Work with your own materials

Respond in chat or your own notes:

  • What would someone need to know?
  • What would they need to have access to?
  • What might confuse or block them?

Work quickly — don't overthink this.

~7 min. Encourage fast responses. If participants don't have materials yet, they can work with their training idea or the example from the previous slide.

Exercise 1: Debrief

What patterns do you see?

  • Prior knowledge — what learners need to already know
  • Tools and access — what they need to have
  • Context — time, language, environment

These are the assumptions sitting between your activity and your outcome.

~8 min. Scan chat and surface 2–3 examples, grouping them into the three categories. Connect to Part 1: "If these don't match your learners or context, your Theory of Change breaks at that point." Key shift: "When you reuse or adapt a resource, these expectations come with it. You are not starting from zero — you are inheriting assumptions." Close: "This is where adaptation starts — not with content, but with expectations."

From assumptions to evaluation

You've looked at what materials expect from learners.

Now: what exists, and does it fit your context?

~30 sec. Brief transition.

Finding and evaluating OER

You are not looking for the "best" resource.

You are looking for something that works for your learners, in your context.

~2 min. Set expectations clearly. Connect to Part 1: "Use the learning outcome you defined yesterday as your anchor. If a resource doesn't help achieve that outcome, it doesn't matter how good it is."

Three quick checks

When you look at a resource, ask:

  • Does this help my learners achieve the course outcomes? (fit)
  • Would someone understand what to do without me? (clarity)
  • Does this assume things that don't match my context? (context)

Most resources will not fit perfectly — and that's expected.

~3 min. Keep this light — a quick check, not a scoring system. "The goal is not to find perfect — it's to decide what could work with changes." Transition: "You'll now look at one resource and quickly judge whether it could work."

Exercise 2: Evaluate a resource

15 min · Individual → whole group

Work with one resource (provided or your own).

~1 min setup. If participants don't have a resource in mind, provide 1–2 examples they can use. This exercise should take 15 min total. If running long, cut debrief to 4 min.

Exercise 2: Individual work

8–10 min

Using your learning outcome from Part 1, make a quick judgement:

  • Does this help my learners achieve the outcome I defined?
  • Would someone understand what to do without me?
  • Does this assume things that don't match my learners or context?

Work quickly — don't overthink this.

If useful, glance at a second resource: which fits better for your context, and why?

~10 min. Encourage quick, instinctive judgements. The goal is comparison and decision-making, not deep analysis.

Exercise 2: Debrief

What patterns did you notice?

  • What tends to fit?
  • What tends to break?
  • Where do assumptions mismatch?

Most resources don't fit perfectly — and that's expected.

~5 min. Scan chat and surface patterns. Connect to Part 1: "Where you see mismatch, that's where your design starts to break. This is the same logic you used in your Theory of Change — but now applied to materials."

Poll: What limits your ability to use a resource?

  • It doesn't fit my learners or outcome
  • It assumes tools, access, or conditions I don't have
  • The license restricts what I need to change
  • I don't have time to adapt it
~5 min total. Launch poll, display results, then ask: "What stands out here?" Take 1–2 short responses. Close: "Some constraints are about fit — and some are about what you are allowed to do." This sets up the licensing framing.

From fit to permission

Sometimes the constraint is not fit —

it's what you are allowed to do with a resource.

~30 sec. Brief transition into licensing.

Understanding open licenses

You may find something that fits your learners well —

but you still need to check what you're allowed to do with it.

~2 min. Build directly on the poll: "We've just seen that sometimes the constraint is not fit — it's what you are allowed to do with a resource." Keep this practical.

The licensing spectrum

~3 min. Use the visual to anchor a simple idea: "Not all resources can be changed." Highlight only the key cases: No license = you cannot reuse or adapt. CC BY = you can reuse and adapt (with attribution). CC BY-ND = you can reuse, but not adapt. Do not expand beyond these three cases.

What you need to know

  • No license → you cannot reuse or adapt
  • CC BY → reuse and adapt, with attribution
  • CC BY-ND → reuse only — no changes allowed

You don't need to memorise licenses.

You need to recognise when something limits what you can do.

~2 min. Reinforce the decision logic. Connect back to the poll: "This is one of the constraints you identified." Close: "At this point, you know what fits, what doesn't, and what you are allowed to change." Transition: "Now apply this to the resource you just evaluated."

Exercise 3: What are you allowed to do?

15 min · Individual → whole group

Return to the resource you evaluated.

~1 min setup. This exercise should take 15 min total. If running long, cut debrief to 5 min.

Exercise 3: Individual work

6–8 min

Identify:

  • What license does it use? (if any)
  • What does this allow you to do?

Then apply it to your situation:

  • Can you reuse it as is?
  • Can you adapt or change it?
  • Does the license limit what you need to do?

If the license is unclear: what risk does that create for you?

~8 min. Encourage participants to work quickly. If they can't find a license, that itself is an important finding.

Exercise 3: Debrief

What did you find?

  • Missing or unclear licenses
  • Restrictions on adaptation
  • Uncertainty about what is allowed

You've now looked at what fits, what doesn't, and what you are allowed to change.

~6 min. Surface patterns from chat. Close the analysis phase: "At this point, you know what is possible — and what is not." Transition: "After the break, we move from analysis to action: how to make something actually work in your context."

Welcome back

So far, we've analysed:

  • What materials assume
  • What fits
  • What you are allowed to do
~2 min. Bring participants back gently. Quick recap of the first half.

From analysis to action

Now we shift:

What would need to change to make something actually work for your learners?

Take 2–3 minutes to think silently.

~3 min. Give participants genuine silence. Do not rush this — it's a reset moment. Transition: "Now we apply this to your own materials."

Adapting, remixing, and localising

You've found something that might work.

You've checked what you're allowed to do.

Now: how do you make it actually work for your learners?

~2 min. Build directly on the reset. Keep this practical.

Three types of change

  • Surface — small edits (wording, examples, formatting)
  • Structural — changing how something is organised or used
  • Contextual — changing assumptions (tools, learners, environment)

Most useful adaptation happens when you change how something works — not just how it looks.

~3 min. Keep examples brief. Connect to Part 1: "These changes are how you address the assumptions you identified in your Theory of Change. This is where you close the gap between your design and your context." Reframe: "You are not improving the resource. You are making it usable for your learners."

Exercise 4: What needs to change?

28 min · Individual → small groups → whole group

Return to the resource you selected earlier.

~1 min setup. This is the longest exercise. If running long, cut debrief to 3 min.

Exercise 4: Individual work

10–12 min

For your selected resource, identify:

  • What would you keep?
  • What would you change?
  • What would you remove or replace?

Don't try to redesign everything — focus on the changes that would make this usable in your context.

Which assumptions from your Theory of Change does this need to address?

~12 min. Encourage focused, practical decisions. If participants don't have a specific resource, they can work with their own draft materials and identify what would need to change for a different audience.

Exercise 4: Small groups

10–12 min · Groups of 2–3

Share your resource and your proposed changes.

Ask each other:

  • What are they missing?
  • What would still break for their learners?

Keep feedback direct and practical.

~12 min. Put participants into breakout rooms of 2–3 people. Each person shares briefly (3–4 min), then the others give feedback. The goal is to surface blind spots — changes they didn't think of.

Exercise 4: Debrief

What patterns emerged?

  • Surface changes are common but often insufficient
  • Structural and contextual changes are where impact happens
  • Assumptions are often not fully addressed

Adaptation is about making something work in your context — not making it better.

~4 min. Invite 2–3 quick shares. Make the key point explicit: "Most useful adaptation happens when you change how something works — not just how it looks." Transition: "If the changes you're identifying start to feel like rewriting everything, the next step is to create something new."

When adaptation isn't enough

If those changes start to feel like rewriting everything,

creating something new may be simpler.

~30 sec. Brief transition.

Creating OER from scratch

Creation makes sense when:

  • Nothing fits your learners or outcome
  • Adaptation becomes too complex
  • Licensing prevents the changes you need
~2 min. Keep this grounded: "Creating does not mean building a full course. It means creating the smallest thing that will work for your learners."

A useful resource works without you

It tells people what to do

It gives enough detail to act

It does not rely on explanation


This should directly support the outcome you defined yesterday.

~3 min. Make this practical. Connect to Part 1. Transition: "You'll now create one small component that someone else could use without you."

Exercise 5: Create a usable component

18 min · Individual → small groups → whole group

~1 min setup. This exercise should take 18 min total. If running long, cut the group work to 4 min and debrief to 2 min.

Exercise 5: Individual work

10–12 min

Create one small component. For example:

  • A short instruction
  • A task or activity
  • A step in a process

Keep it small and focused.

Someone else should be able to use this without asking you for clarification.

~12 min. Encourage participants to keep it small — one piece, not everything. Remind them: include enough detail so it is clear what to do and what the outcome should be. Connect to Part 1: "This should directly support the outcome you defined yesterday."

Exercise 5: Small groups

5–6 min · Groups of 2–3

Share what you created. Ask:

  • Could you use this without the creator explaining it?
  • What is unclear or missing?

Keep feedback direct and specific.

~6 min. Put participants into breakout rooms of 2–3. Each person shares briefly, then gets feedback. The focus is on usability — can someone else actually use this?

Exercise 5: Debrief

  • What made something usable?
  • What made something unclear?

If someone needs you to explain it, it's not ready yet.

~2 min. Invite 2–3 quick reflections. Reinforce the core idea. Transition: "Now we look at what others would need to use and share your materials."

From creating to sharing

You've created something that works without you.

Can someone else find it, understand it, and use it?

~30 sec. Brief transition.

Sharing and publishing OER

What others need from your materials:

  • Clarity — what is this and what is it for?
  • Instructions — what should someone do?
  • Context — who is this for and how should it be used?

Sharing does not mean publishing perfectly.

It means making something usable beyond your immediate context.

~4 min. Keep this practical and grounded. Reinforce: "If someone can understand and use it without you, it is ready to share." Transition: "Take what you created and make it easier for someone else to use."

Exercise 6: Make it easier to use

13 min · Individual → chat → whole group

~1 min setup. This exercise should take 13 min total. If running long, cut debrief to 2 min.

Exercise 6: Individual work

6–7 min

Return to the component you created. Make it easier for someone else to use by improving:

  • Clarity — what is this and what is it for?
  • Instructions — what should someone do?
  • Context — who is this for and how should it be used?

Keep changes small and focused.

Does this clearly support the outcome you defined yesterday?

~7 min. Remind participants: "You are not redesigning — you are making it easier to understand and use."

Exercise 6: Share in chat

3–4 min

Post one improvement you made.

~4 min. Let participants share, then scan for patterns.

Exercise 6: Debrief

  • What helped make things clearer?
  • What was commonly missing?

Small changes can make a resource much easier to reuse.

~2 min. Surface patterns from chat. Reinforce the key point. Transition: "Once something is shared, the next step is how it changes over time."

Sustaining and improving OER

Once something is shared, it doesn't stay fixed.

People will interpret it differently, adapt it further, and find gaps.

~2 min. Keep this grounded in reality.

The OER lifecycle

graph LR A[Create / Adapt] --> B[Share] B --> C[Use] C --> D[Gather feedback] D --> E[Improve] E --> B

A resource becomes more useful over time if it is used, tested, and improved.

~3 min. Keep this practical: "You do not need to maintain everything. You can decide to update it, leave it as it is, or stop using it." Connect to Part 1: "This is part of working in a real system — things change, and your materials need to respond to that." Close the loop: "The goal is not to create something perfect. It's to create something that can be used, adapted, and improved."

finding → evaluating → adapting → creating → sharing
