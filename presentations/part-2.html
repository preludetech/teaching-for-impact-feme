The shift

Part 1 was about designing training for your learners.

Part 2 is about making those materials work for people who were not in the room when you designed them.

Can your materials stand on their own?

Think about the materials you designed in Part 1, or your existing course

Could a colleague pick them up and run the session without you?

~2 min. Let the questions land. Most people's honest answer is "probably not" — that's the starting point.

Every resource carries assumptions

Remember the story about teaching women to make websites?

The instructions said "open a text editor" — and many participants didn't know what that meant.

The assumption was invisible to the people who wrote the materials.

Assumptions aren't problems. Invisible assumptions are.

~3 min. Connect to the Part 1 story. The assumptions you built into your design — about your learners, their context, their constraints — are the same ones that shape how reusable your materials are. When someone can see the assumptions, they can decide whether to accept, adapt, or replace them.

Exercise 1: Hidden Assumptions Audit

25 min · Whole group

One team shares a piece of their training materials on screen. Everyone examines it together through three questions:

  1. What does the reader need to already know?
  2. What does the reader need to have? (tools, software, connectivity)
  3. What context is baked in? (examples, cultural references, institutional assumptions)
~5 min setup + 25 min group discussion. Ask for a volunteer team (or choose one). They share a piece of their training materials — an activity, a slide deck, a set of instructions. This can be something from Part 1. The facilitator captures assumptions as the group calls them out. Participants from other teams often spot assumptions the authoring team cannot see — that's the point.

Exercise 1: Narrowing down

From all the assumptions we found:

  • Which 2–3 would cause the most confusion if someone missed them?
  • Which can be solved with better materials?
  • How can identifying these up front make your content more reusable?
~5 min wrap-up. Guide the group to identify the highest-impact assumptions. Note that some (like "the learner understands X") can be addressed with better documentation, while others (like "the learner has reliable internet") are conditions to state, not solve. Ask the final question to connect this to openness.

Exercise 1: Debrief

What types of assumptions came up most?

  • Tool or software assumptions?
  • Language or background knowledge?
  • Institutional or cultural context?

Making assumptions visible is one of the simplest things you can do to support openness.

~5 min. Draw out patterns. The key insight: even a short note — "This activity assumes learners have basic spreadsheet skills" — transforms a hidden barrier into a visible design choice others can work with. This is the foundation for everything in Part 2.

From assumptions to permissions

You've started seeing what your materials assume.

Before we evaluate other people's materials, we need to understand one more thing: what are you allowed to do with them?

~30 sec. Brief transition into the licensing primer.

Open licenses: the basics

A resource being freely available online does not mean you can modify or redistribute it.

By default, copyright reserves all rights to the creator.

Open licenses change that default — they say, in advance, "here is what you're allowed to do."

~2 min. Keep this brisk. The goal is enough understanding to inform evaluation decisions in Exercise 2, not comprehensive licensing expertise.

Creative Commons: four building blocks

  • BY (Attribution) — credit the creator
  • SA (ShareAlike) — adaptations must use the same license
  • NC (NonCommercial) — no commercial use
  • ND (NoDerivatives) — no modifications allowed
~2 min. These four elements combine into six standard licenses. BY appears in every CC license. SA and ND are the restrictive ones — SA constrains how you license your adaptation; ND blocks adaptation entirely.

The CC license spectrum

~2 min. Walk through the spectrum briefly. For training materials, CC BY and CC BY-SA are the most useful — they allow the adaptation and resharing that make OER practical. ND prevents adaptation entirely, which limits usefulness for training design.

Practical rules of thumb

  • No license = no permission. Treat it as fully copyrighted
  • ND blocks adaptation. You can share it as-is, but you can't modify it
  • SA sets the license for your output. Your combined work must use the same license
  • "Free to use" is not a license. Look for a specific CC license
~2 min. These rules are enough to make evaluation decisions. Point participants to Lesson 3 in the workbook for the full picture on compatibility. Quick worked example: "If you find a CC BY-SA resource and adapt it, your adaptation must also be CC BY-SA. That's fine — it just means your version stays open."

Writing correct attribution

Four elements:

  1. Creator — who made the original
  2. Title — the name of the work
  3. Source — where it can be found
  4. License — what license applies
Adapted from "Introduction to Qualitative Coding" by J. Mwangi, available at [link]. Licensed under CC BY 4.0. Adapted by [your name]: examples replaced with community health scenarios.
~2 min. Show the example. The key message: attribution is not bureaucracy — it's how the open ecosystem works. When you credit clearly, you make it easier for others to find and build on the same materials.

Three ways to build training materials

~2 min. Most effective training combines all three approaches. The question is not "which one?" but "which one for which part?" — and that depends on what you find.

Six lenses for evaluation

  1. Alignment — does it match your outcomes?
  2. Clarity — could your learners follow it without heavy explanation?
  3. Context fit — do the examples work for your learners?
  4. Adaptability — can you actually edit and modify it?
  5. Accessibility — can your learners access it? (devices, bandwidth, language)
  6. Licensing — are you allowed to adapt and share it?
~3 min. Walk through each lens briefly. The key insight: evaluation is not about quality in the abstract — it's about fit for your specific purpose. A polished resource can still fail in your context.

Exercise 2: Evaluate, Then Decide

Phase 1 — Six-lens evaluation

15 min · Own-team breakout

Pick one resource you brought from the pre-work (Step 5). Evaluate it using the six lenses:

  1. Alignment
  2. Clarity
  3. Context fit
  4. Adaptability
  5. Accessibility
  6. Licensing

For each lens, rate: green (works), amber (needs work), red (deal-breaker)

~5 min setup + 15 min breakout. If a team didn't complete pre-work Step 5, provide one of the curated fallback resources — the goal is evaluation, not searching. Facilitator prep: have 3–4 curated OER examples ready as fallbacks, chosen for a mix of quality levels across the six lenses.

Exercise 2: Evaluate, Then Decide

Phase 2 — Reuse, adapt, or create?

8 min · Own-team breakout (continue from Phase 1)

Based on your evaluation, make a decision:

  • What did you decide? (reuse / adapt / create)
  • Why? What drove the decision?
  • If adapting: What level? Surface, structural, or contextual?
  • If creating: Why wasn't adaptation enough?

Capture your decision alongside the evaluation.

~8 min. Teams stay in the same breakout — Phase 2 flows directly from Phase 1. The adaptation-level framing (surface/structural/contextual) gives teams vocabulary for scoping the work ahead.

Exercise 2: Debrief

What was the lens that surprised you most?

The one where you expected green but got amber or red?

~4 min. Draw out the pattern that accessibility and licensing are commonly overlooked. These are the lenses that most often surprise teams.

Quick poll

How many teams chose…

  • Reuse as-is?
  • Adapt?
  • Create from scratch?

Adaptation is the most common answer — and that's normal.

~3 min. Show of hands or chat poll. Normalise all three choices. If anyone chose "create," ask briefly what made adaptation insufficient — this previews the creation mindset from Lesson 5.

Break

Back at [TIME]

20 minutes

State the actual return time. E.g., "We'll be back at 2:30."

From deciding to doing

You've evaluated a resource and decided to adapt it.

Now you turn that decision into concrete changes.

~30 sec. Brief transition back from break.

Three levels of adaptation

  • Surface — terminology, tone, formatting, images
  • Structural — reordering, splitting, changing activity format
  • Contextual — replacing examples, substituting tools, rethinking prerequisites

Most adaptations involve all three.

~3 min. Walk through each level with a quick example. Surface: changing "fall semester" to "Term 2." Structural: splitting a 90-minute session into two 45-minute blocks. Contextual: replacing a European case study with one relevant to your region. The point: understanding the level helps you gauge effort and risk.

Adaptation is targeted, not total

You don't need to change everything.

For each mismatch, decide:

  • Keep — it works, or the gap is minor
  • Modify — specific changes, core structure stays
  • Remove — it doesn't serve your outcomes
  • Replace — the purpose is right but the content needs to be different
~2 min. Focus effort on changes that make the biggest difference for your learners. A localised example that helps learners connect is worth more than perfect formatting.

Exercise 3: Adaptation Deep Dive

35 min · Own-team breakout

Take the resource you decided to adapt in Exercise 2. Work through it section by section and identify:

  • Surface changes — terminology, tone, formatting
  • Structural changes — reordering, splitting, removing sections
  • Contextual changes — replacing examples, substituting tools, adjusting prerequisites
~5 min setup + 35 min activity. Teams work on the resource they evaluated in Exercise 2. If a team chose "create" rather than "adapt," they can work on planning what they'll create — but encourage them to find at least one existing resource as a reference point. This exercise should take 45 min total including debrief. If running long, cut debrief to 3 min.

Exercise 3: Adaptation note

For your most significant change, draft a brief note:

  • What you changed
  • Why you changed it
  • What remains context-specific
This documents your reasoning — for others who might adapt your version, and for your future self.
Keep this slide visible during the breakout. The adaptation note models the documentation that makes OER genuinely reusable. Even a few sentences per significant change is enough — the goal is transparency, not bureaucracy.

Exercise 3: Debrief

What was your team's most significant adaptation decision?

  • Was it surface, structural, or contextual?
  • What drove the decision?
~5 min. Ask 2–3 teams to share their key decision. Listen for whether teams are distinguishing between the three levels. If all answers are surface-level, probe: "Did anyone need to change the structure or replace examples entirely?"

The real test

You've audited assumptions, evaluated resources, and planned adaptations.

Now: does your material actually work for someone who's never seen it?

~30 sec. Build anticipation. This is the most powerful exercise in the session.

The stranger test

Imagine a competent colleague in a different country, working in a related field, who has never met you. Can they understand what to do? Can they see why each activity matters? Can they adapt the materials without guessing at your intentions?

You're about to find out.

~2 min. Read the quote or paraphrase it. The point: "writing for people you'll never meet" is about to become viscerally concrete.

Exercise 4: Stranger Test Swap

Setup

Each team selects one piece of their training materials — ideally the one you audited in Exercise 1.

Share it with a different team via screen-share or file share.

~5 min setup. Assign the cross-team pairings. Each team sends their materials to a different team. Keep it practical — screen-share or a quick file share, whatever works.

Exercise 4a: Review

10 min · Cross-team breakout

Read through the other team's materials and try to answer:

  1. What is this material for? What are learners supposed to learn or do?
  2. Could you run this activity with your own learners? What would you need to change?
  3. Where did you get stuck or confused? What was missing?
~10 min. The reviewing team works through the materials as if encountering them for the first time. Encourage them to note specific moments of confusion, not general impressions. This exercise should take 30 min total. If running long, shorten the paired feedback to 7 min.

Exercise 4b: Paired feedback

10 min · Cross-team breakout

The reviewing team talks to the team that created the materials:

  • Share your answers to the three questions
  • Creating team: listen and note the gaps

This is not a critique — it's a gift. You're hearing exactly what a stranger experiences.

~10 min. Teams pair up on a call. The reviewing team shares, the creating team listens. Remind the creating team: resist the urge to explain. If you need to explain something, that's the gap.

Exercise 4c: Harvest

5 min · Whole group

What was the most common gap — what did reviewers need that wasn't in the materials?

~5 min. This harvest doubles as the debrief. Ask 3–4 groups to share the biggest gap. Common answers: missing context, unclear purpose, assumed knowledge not stated, missing facilitator instructions. The discussion itself surfaces the key insights — don't over-summarise.

Closing: One thing I'll change

3 min · Individual reflection

Write down one specific thing you will change about your training materials based on today.

Not a vague intention — a concrete action.

"I will add a prerequisites note to my data cleaning activity."

"I will check the license on the dataset I've been using."

"I will rewrite my activity instructions so someone else could run them."

~3 min. Give a full 3 minutes of quiet writing time. The specificity requirement is the whole point — vague commitments don't lead to action.

Share within teams

5 min · Own-team breakout

Each person shares their "one thing."

Are there common themes across the team?

~5 min. Teams discuss. Common themes often emerge: most teams realise they need better documentation, clearer assumptions, or more explicit instructions.

Harvest

Who wants to share their "one thing"?

~5 min. Ask 4–5 participants to share. Facilitator notes patterns. Then transition into the close.

The OER lifecycle

graph LR A[Create / Adapt] --> B[Share] B --> C[Use] C --> D[Gather feedback] D --> E[Improve] E --> B

What you've described is the beginning of this cycle — not a one-time fix.

~2 min. Connect participants' commitments to the OER lifecycle. The "one thing" they'll change is their entry point into the cycle. Ask: "What will you do with these materials after your next delivery?" This plants the sustainability seed (outcomes 27–30) without a dedicated exercise. Don't rush this — the closing question is doing double duty (concrete commitment + introducing ongoing improvement).

What will you do after your next delivery?

Collect feedback. Notice what you had to explain. Update what confused people.

Your materials enter a cycle of use, feedback, and improvement.

That's what makes them open — not a license, but a practice.

~2 min. This is the sustainability message. Since there's no dedicated sustainability exercise, this is where the OER lifecycle concept lands. The key reframe: openness is a practice, not a feature you add.
