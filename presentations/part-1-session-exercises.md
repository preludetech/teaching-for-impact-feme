# Part 1 Workshop: Session Plan

## Summary

- **Total session time:** 4 hours allocated (240 minutes). Scheduled content runs ~3 hours 27 minutes (including one 20-minute break), leaving ~33 minutes buffer for Q&A overruns.
- **Content time:** ~3 hours 7 minutes
- **Exercises:** 5 core + 1 stretch + 3 alternatives available
- **Stretch exercise:** Exercise 6 (Learner Experience Audit) runs if the session is ahead of schedule. If it's cut, extend the closing reflection to 10 min.
- **If running long:** The 33-minute buffer absorbs most overruns. If the buffer is consumed, limit Exercise 5's live share to 2 teams instead of 3 (reclaims ~4 min). If still tight, compress the closing reflection from 10 to 5 min.
- **Format:** Remote video call with breakout rooms. No shared canvas or collaborative tools — exercises rely on conversation and screen-sharing only
- **Participants:** Teams developing their own training. Grouping varies per exercise (own team vs cross-team mix)
- **Team size:** 3–5 people per team. If a team has only 1–2 members, pair them with another small team for own-team exercises. If a team has 6+, split them into sub-groups for breakout work and reunite for whole-group phases.
- **Capturing outputs:** Teams produce artefacts across exercises (system map, theory of change chain, learning outcomes, activity design). Remind teams at the start to keep their own notes — there is no shared workspace. Outputs from earlier exercises feed into later ones, so if they don't save their work it's lost when breakout rooms close.

## Session flow (in order of execution)

> **Note on framing sections:** The framing sections before exercises 1–3 are longer and more interactive than those later in the session. Early on, participants need more facilitator guidance and whole-group connection before they're ready for productive breakout work. By the afternoon, they're warmed up and can move into exercises more quickly.

---

### Opening warm-up (0:00–0:15)

**Format:** Whole-group welcome → short cross-team breakout → whole-group share

**Grouping:** Starts whole-group (so participants settle in with the facilitator), then cross-team breakout for the icebreaker conversation.

The facilitator opens in the main room (5 min): welcome, set the tone — this is a working session, not a lecture. Make it clear that the facilitator is a co-thinker, not an authority dispensing answers. Keep the welcome brief; don't try to have everyone introduce themselves in the main room — with 20+ participants, that eats all the time and kills energy.

There must be a slide introducing Talarify, Anelda, Sheena, and Prelude.

Then participants go to cross-team breakout rooms (3–4 people from different teams) for 7 minutes. In the breakout, each person introduces themselves and briefly describes their own project — who they're training, what skill or topic they're focused on, and where things stand.

Back in the main room (5 min), the facilitator runs a quick think-and-share. Put the question on screen: "What's one thing about your training design that you're most uncertain about?" Give 1 minute of silent thinking time — let people actually formulate a thought before anyone speaks. Then ask 3–4 people to share. The facilitator actively names recurring themes as they emerge — these become reference points throughout the workshop ("Several of you mentioned unclear objectives — that's exactly what Exercise 4 addresses"). This shows participants the workshop is responding to their experience, not running on rails.

Close the warm-up with a brief framing of the workshop arc: "Today we're working through the design process from the outside in — starting with the system your training lives in, then zooming in to your learners, your outcomes, and finally the activities themselves. Everything we do today, you'll do with your own training in mind."

---

### Framing: Training lives in a system (0:15–0:22, 7 min, whole-group)

Don't just say "training lives in a system" — unpack it. Ask the whole group: "Think of a training that didn't work — yours or one you attended. What went wrong? Was it the content, or something around the content?" Take 2–3 answers. Use these to build the idea that design problems are often system problems — the content was fine but the timing was wrong, the learners didn't have the prerequisites, the managers weren't on board, the internet went down.

Example: I was once running some teamwork training for a large team. The training required very small amounts of daily effort to build habits. Participants were already being pulled in many directions at work, so even small additions overwhelmed them. The training had to be adapted — smaller daily requirements, clearer priorities for when time was short.

Briefly remind participants what a system map includes: actors, resources, constraints, and connections. They've already gathered context through the pre-work — this is a quick orientation, not a first encounter.

End with the task setup: "In your teams, you're going to map the system around your own training."

### Exercise 1: System Map — core (0:22–0:41)

**Original activity:** Activity 1: System Map (Lesson 1)

**Grouping:** Own team — they map the system around the training they're developing together, so this grounds all later exercises in their shared project.

**Adapted instructions:**
Teams already drafted individual system maps in the pre-work. In their breakout room, they compare maps and build a shared version — filling gaps, challenging assumptions, and surfacing elements others missed. They should push beyond the obvious: who affects the training after learners leave the room? What resources are fragile? What connections did no one spot alone? One person can sketch or take notes on their own screen, but the point is the conversation, not the artefact.

After 10 minutes, teams return to the main room. One volunteer team presents their system map highlights — screen-sharing or talking through it (2 min). The facilitator then opens the floor: "What's missing? What connections do you see that the team might not?" (3 min). Facilitator closes with the key takeaway: systems thinking means your design decisions don't stop at the classroom door (2 min).

**Time:** 19 min (2 min setup / 10 min team discussion in breakout rooms / 7 min "one team goes deep" whole-group debrief)

**Reason for inclusion:** This is the foundational exercise. It immediately grounds each team in their own context and establishes the core insight of Part 1 — that training is an intervention in a system, not a standalone event. Working as a team means they surface different perspectives on the same system. The conversation generates shared understanding that feeds into later exercises.

**Outcomes addressed:** L1.1 (describe training as system intervention), L1.2 (identify system elements), L6.2 (identify constraints — lightly)

### Framing: How does training lead to change? (0:41–0:48, 7 min, whole-group)

possible story: A friend of mine was made to do a Python programming course as a part of a job. She's not a programmer, but her job is for work that Python could definitely help make her more efficient. The course didn't land, no changes were made. My friend and her entire department have been put through multiple Python training courses that have had no effect on their daily work. Why? Course aimed to get them to understand basic Python. The desired effect was that they would apply their Python skills at their jobs going forward.

Then introduce the theory-of-change chain by building it for the Python story together. The chain has five links — walk through each one briefly. Participants have already thought about long-term impact through the pre-work, so this is about introducing the chain structure, not the concept of impact:

- **Inputs** — the resources you bring to the training: trainers, materials, budget, time, technology, venue. These are what you invest.
- **Activities** — what happens during the training itself: lectures, exercises, discussions, group projects. These are what participants *do*.
- **Outputs** — the tangible, countable things participants produce or complete during the training: a finished exercise, a submitted assignment, a working script, a completed checklist. Outputs are evidence that activities happened — you can point at them and count them. They don't tell you whether anyone *learned* anything.
- **Outcomes** — the changes in participants' behaviour, practice, or capability *after* the training. This is where learning actually shows up in the real world. An outcome is something like "participants routinely use Python to automate repetitive data tasks in their daily work." Outcomes happen outside the training room and over time.
- **Impact** — the broader, longer-term change the training contributes to: organisational efficiency, better health outcomes, stronger research capacity. Impact is rarely caused by training alone — your training contributes to it alongside other factors.

The chain flows like this: Inputs → Activities → Outputs → Outcomes → Impact. Each arrow represents an assumption — something that has to be true for one link to lead to the next.

Build the chain for the Python story together: "So the impact they wanted was efficiency gains. What outcome in the learners would lead to that? What outputs would show progress during the training? What activities and inputs?" Walk through the chain live, showing how it looks logical on paper — and then ask: "So where did it actually break?"

Name the key move: "The chain looks logical. But every arrow hides an assumption. Your outcomes don't automatically lead to your impact — something has to be true for that link to hold. That's what we're going to hunt for."

Set up the exercise: teams build their own chain first, then others will try to break it.

### Exercise 2: Theory of Change (Lite) — core (0:48–1:12)

**Original activity:** Activity 2: Theory of Change (Lesson 2)

**Grouping:** Own team for Phase 1 (building the chain), then cross-team mix for Phase 2 (assumption-hunting on another team's map). IMportant to frame things as non competitive. A fresh set of eyes can spot holes you didnt so you are more likely to succeed. Goal is not to attack, but to support and be curious.

**Adapted instructions:**
Phase 1 — Build your chain (10 min, own team breakout): Teams go to a breakout room and talk through the theory-of-change chain for their training: Impact ← Outcomes ← Outputs ← Activities ← Inputs. They start from Impact and work backwards. The goal is to get the chain on screen — one person sketches or types while the team talks. Don't aim for perfection; aim for a first draft that's concrete enough for someone else to read.

Phase 2 — Hunt for assumptions (6 min, cross-team breakout): Breakout rooms are reshuffled so each room has members from different teams. Each person briefly walks the others through their team's chain. The reviewers focus on the right-hand side — outcomes and impact — and ask: "What has to be true for this step to lead to the next? What could break here?" They name assumptions the team may have taken for granted. Each reviewer marks or notes the assumption they think is weakest.

Whole-group debrief (5 min): One reviewer presents the weakest assumption they found in someone else's chain — not just names it, but explains why it's fragile and what could break (1.5 min). The facilitator models the next move with the whole group: "OK, so this assumption is shaky. What would we change in the design to reduce the risk?" Whole group brainstorms briefly (2 min). The facilitator reinforces the key insight: assumptions aren't bad — unexamined ones are dangerous (1.5 min).

**Time:** 24 min (3 min setup / 10 min own-team chain building in breakout / 6 min cross-team assumption-hunting in breakout / 5 min facilitator-led whole-group debrief)

**Reason for inclusion:** Theory of Change is a powerful concept that many researchers encounter for the first time here. Splitting the exercise into own-team building and cross-team review serves two purposes: teams get to focus on their own chain without distraction in Phase 1, then get the benefit of fresh eyes in Phase 2. Outsiders are better at spotting assumptions — especially around outcomes and impact, where teams tend to be most optimistic about their own logic. The cross-team format also builds relationships across projects early in the workshop.

**Outcomes addressed:** L2.1 (articulate a theory of change), L2.2 (analyse how context influences design), L2.3 (identify assumptions and risks)

### Framing: Know your learners (1:12–1:18, 6 min, whole-group)

This is where the workshop pivots from system-level thinking to learner-level thinking. Mark the shift explicitly: "We've mapped the system and the logic. Now we zoom in on the people. Everything you design will land — or not — based on how well it fits the humans who show up."

Share one sharp contrast between design assumptions and reality — drawn from the facilitator's own experience. For example: "We were teaching basic web development to women online. The instructions said 'open a text editor' — and a lot of participants didn't know what that meant. We hadn't met the learners up front, and we'd aimed the content at a certain level of computer literacy. But when the course was advertised, a different audience showed up. We ended up spending a lot of extra time dealing with that mismatch and reworking the content." Participants have already thought about their learners through the pre-work — this is about sharpening the lens, not introducing the idea.

Put the four discussion prompts on screen and give 2 minutes of silent thinking time before sending participants to breakout rooms.

### Exercise 3: Learner Reality Check — core (1:18–1:38)

**Original activity:** Activity 3: Learner Reality Mapping (Lesson 3)

**Grouping:** Own-team breakout rooms — teams discuss the prompts together since they share the same learners.

**Adapted instructions:**
Think-then-discuss structure. Facilitator puts four prompts on screen before sending people to breakout rooms:

1. What do they already know or do that your training can build on?
2. What is the single biggest barrier to their participation or success?
3. What does "success" look like from *their* perspective — not yours, not the funder's?
4. What else do you know about your learners as humans — what do they worry about, struggle with, or juggle outside of your training?

Participants go to own-team breakout rooms for a short discussion (10 min). Teams work through the prompts together, focusing on prompts 2 and 3. The key question to wrestle with: "Is that barrier something your design can reduce, or a condition you need to design within?"

Whole-group share (5 min): facilitator asks a few teams to name the most common barrier type that came up. Facilitator groups these on screen into categories (access, time, language, power, motivation). These become shared reference points for the rest of the workshop.

**Time:** 20 min (1 min setup / 10 min own-team breakout / 5 min whole-group collection / 4 min debrief + transition)

**Reason for inclusion:** This is where the workshop pivots from system-level thinking to learner-level thinking. Own-team discussion lets participants build a shared picture of their learners, while the whole-group share surfaces patterns across projects. The barrier-collection creates a shared vocabulary the group uses for the rest of the session.

**Outcomes addressed:** L3.1 (analyse learner characteristics and realities), L3.2 (identify contextual factors), L3.4 (recognise prior knowledge), L3.5 (identify barriers and strategies to reduce them), L3.3 (needs assessment — lightly, through the prompt structure)

### Mini-lecture: How people actually learn (2:03–2:15, 12 min, interactive whole-group)

Present three core principles: cognitive load (your learners' working memory is smaller than you think), retrieval practice (learning happens when people pull knowledge out, not when you push it in — and the more ways they retrieve it, the stronger it sticks. Ask them to apply knowledge in a new context and they have to pull it out and look at it from a different angle, which builds richer mental frameworks and deeper memory), and social learning (people learn by talking, arguing, and building on each other's ideas). For each principle, pause and ask: "Where have you seen this — or its opposite — in a training you've attended or run?" Take 1–2 quick responses per principle.


Address the learning styles myth interactively. Ask the room: "Who has heard of learning styles?" (hands go up). "Who here is a visual learner?" (hands). "Who is an auditory learner?" (hands). Then challenge it: "If you're an auditory learner, could you learn to drive a car by listening to someone talk about it? If you're a visual learner, could you learn to play guitar by watching videos and looking at pictures?" Let the room sit with that for a moment. The point: learning styles are a wasteful myth. The evidence doesn't support matching instruction to styles. What does work is varying how you engage learners — not because of their 'type,' but because different activities build different kinds of understanding.

### Exercise 4: Writing Learning Outcomes — core (2:15–2:38)

**Original activity:** Activity 5: Learning Outcomes (Lesson 5)

**Grouping:** Own team first (drafting), then cross-team pairs (critique) — teams draft outcomes for their shared training, then get fresh-eyes feedback from someone on a different project.

**Adapted instructions:**
Framing (3 min, whole group): Facilitator shows one sharp example of a vague vs strong outcome side by side. Key message: outcomes describe what learners will *do*, not what the training will *cover*. "Understand climate data" vs "Interpret a local dataset to identify two practical risks and explain them to a non-specialist audience." Participants have already attempted drafting outcomes in the pre-work, so this is a quick calibration, not an introduction.

Phase 1 — Team drafting (7 min): teams go to breakout rooms and draft 2–3 learning outcomes for their training. They use this checklist: Is it observable? Does it use an action verb? Is it realistic for our learners?

Phase 2 — Cross-team critique (8 min): breakout rooms are reshuffled so each room has members from different teams. Each person reads out their team's outcomes. The others check: (a) could you observe a learner achieving this — how would you know? (b) is any outcome actually a topic description disguised as an outcome? Discuss and note revisions.

Whole-group debrief (5 min): facilitator asks for 1–2 "before and after" examples — an outcome that improved through the cross-team critique. Uses these to reinforce the observable/action-oriented principle.

**Time:** 23 min (3 min framing / 7 min team drafting in breakout / 8 min cross-team critique in breakout / 5 min debrief)

**Reason for inclusion:** Writing good learning outcomes is the single most transferable skill from this workshop. Everything else in training design — activities, assessment, alignment — depends on outcomes being clear. The cross-team critique gives feedback from people who don't share the team's assumptions — they'll spot vague outcomes the team has been reading as clear.

**Outcomes addressed:** L5.1 (define clear, observable outcomes), L5.2 (apply backward design — working from what learners should do), L5.3 (identify what activity addresses an outcome — through the "how would you observe this?" question), L9.3 (define observable indicators — implicitly, through the observability check)

The activity is not a topic — it's a thing learners do that lets them practise the skill they are meant to achieve."

### Exercise 5: Start Designing an Activity — core (2:43–3:17)

**Original activity:** Activity 8: Learning Activity Design (Lesson 7)

**Grouping:** Own team for design, then whole-group share and discussion — teams begin designing together because it's their training, then hear how others approached the same challenge.

**Adapted instructions:**
This is the integrative exercise — teams apply everything from the morning to begin designing a learning activity. The goal is not a finished product; it's a solid starting point they can develop further after the workshop.

Phase 1 — Design (15 min, own team breakout): Each team selects one learning outcome from Exercise 4. They begin designing an activity that helps learners achieve that outcome. The facilitator puts the six-component template on screen before sending teams to breakout rooms. Teams talk through as many components as they can:

1. **Objective** — which outcome does this address?
2. **Instructions** — step-by-step, clear enough that another facilitator could run it
3. **Materials** — what do learners need?
4. **Time** — how long, broken into stages
5. **Output** — what do learners produce? (must be something concrete)
6. **Participation** — how is everyone involved, not just the confident ones?

Teams won't get through all six — that's expected. Prioritise **Objective**, **Instructions**, and **Output**. These three give enough shape to share meaningfully and to build on later.

Phase 2 — Live share and discussion (15 min, whole group): Back in the main room, 2–3 teams briefly walk through their emerging design (3–4 min each). After each share, the facilitator opens the floor for quick observations: What's strong about this design? What would you want to think through further? The facilitator draws out connections to earlier exercises — "Notice how their output maps directly to the outcome they wrote in Exercise 4."

Wrap-up (2 min): Facilitator reinforces the key message: "You've started something real. You don't need to finish it today — the workbook's six-component template is there for when you pick this up again. The point was to experience what it feels like to design from outcomes, not from topics."

**Time:** 34 min (2 min setup / 15 min team design in breakout / 15 min whole-group share and discussion / 2 min wrap-up)

**Reason for inclusion:** This is where teams move from thinking about design principles to actually doing design. The six-component template gives structure without being prescriptive. Sharing designs in plenary lets the whole room learn from each team's choices and surfaces common challenges. Teams leave with a concrete starting point they can develop further using the workbook.

**Outcomes addressed:** L7.1 (design outcome-aligned activities), L7.2 (structure with clear components), L7.3 (design for inclusive participation), L7.4 (enable co-creation — through the "participation" prompt), L7.6 (adapt to context — through constraints awareness from Exercise 3)

### Closing reflection (3:17–3:27)

**Format:** Whole-group share (no breakout — this is a reflective, connective moment)

**Time:** 10 min (when Exercise 6 is not included) / 5 min (when Exercise 6 runs)

Facilitator asks each team to answer one question aloud: "What is one thing you will do differently in your next training design because of today?" Go round — one answer per team. For the 10-min version, the facilitator draws out the reasoning: "What specifically from today changed your thinking on that?" This connects answers back to specific exercises and reinforces the learning.

End with: "The goal today was not to master all of this — it was to see the full picture and find the parts that matter most for your own work. The workbook is where you go deeper."

Facilitator notes down the commitments — these are valuable feedback for planning follow-up sessions or workbook revisions.

### Exercise 6: Learner Experience Audit — stretch (3:17–3:37)

**Original activity:** Activity 4: Learner Experience Audit (Lesson 4)

**Grouping:** Cross-team mix — auditing someone else's flawed design is more fun and more honest with people who aren't invested in it.

**Facilitator prep:** Create the flawed training segment before the workshop. This should be a 1-page session plan (15–20 min activity) with clear but realistic problems: a long passive lecture block, vague instructions, no learner output, missed opportunities for peer interaction, and cognitive overload from too many new concepts at once. Base it on a plausible domain (e.g., a data literacy session) so it feels real but isn't anyone's actual work. Have it ready to screen-share.

**Adapted instructions:**
Facilitator screen-shares the flawed training segment. Cross-team breakout rooms of 3–4 people audit it against three questions (10 min):

1. Where would learners feel overwhelmed — and is that the material's fault or the design's fault?
2. Where are learners passive for more than 10 minutes — and what could they do instead?
3. Where could learners learn from each other — and what structure would make that work?

Each group agrees on 2 concrete improvements. Back in the main room, facilitator harvests the best improvements (5 min) and asks: "Which of these problems do you recognise in your own training designs?" (3 min).

**Time:** 20 min (2 min setup / 10 min cross-team breakout audit / 5 min whole-group harvest / 3 min reflection)

**Reason for inclusion:** This is a high-energy exercise that makes learning science principles concrete and diagnostic. Participants actively hunt for design problems — cognitive overload, passivity, missed opportunities for social learning — rather than discussing them abstractly. The mini-lecture on how people learn (earlier in the session) provides the conceptual foundation; this exercise gives participants a chance to apply those ideas. Placing it after Exercise 5 means participants have just designed their own activity, so the "which of these problems do you recognise in your own designs?" question lands with real force.

**Outcomes addressed:** L4.1 (explain learning principles), L4.2 (identify factors supporting/hindering learning), L4.3 (apply cognitive load strategies), L4.4 (retrieval and reinforcement), L4.5 (social learning)

## Alternative exercises

### Alternative A: Co-Design Discussion

**Original activity:** Activity 7: Co-Design Plan (Lesson 6)

**Could replace:** Exercise 3 (Learner Reality Check). Not recommended as a replacement for Exercise 2 (Theory of Change) — that exercise is now core and feeds assumption-awareness into later exercises.

**Grouping:** Cross-team mix — hearing how other teams navigate constraints and power dynamics surfaces approaches they wouldn't think of alone.

**Tradeoff:** You lose the detailed learner analysis and barrier mapping, but gain a focus on who shapes design decisions and whether participation is meaningful. Not recommended — learner reality is more foundational and feeds directly into later exercises.

**Adapted instructions:**
Cross-team breakout rooms of 3–4 people. Facilitator reads a scenario before sending people to rooms (e.g., "The funder has fixed the topic and timeline. Where can participants still have real influence?"). Groups discuss for 10 min, then return to the main room. Facilitator asks 2–3 groups to present their recommendation and the trade-off they made. Whole-group discussion (5 min) on the difference between genuine co-design and performative consultation.

**Time:** 20 min (2 min setup / 10 min cross-team breakout discussion / 5 min presentations / 3 min whole-group discussion)

**Outcomes addressed:** L6.1 (co-design principles), L6.2 (constraints), L6.3 (trade-offs), L6.4 (meaningful participation), L6.5 (avoid extractive practices), L2.2 (positionality — through the power analysis in the scenarios)

### Alternative B: Alignment Quick-Check

**Original activity:** Activity 6: Alignment Table (Lesson 5), simplified

**Could replace:** Exercise 6 (Learner Experience Audit)

**Grouping:** Own team — they check alignment on the activity they just designed together.

**Tradeoff:** You lose the hands-on learning-science diagnostic, but gain an explicit alignment check that connects outcomes, activities, and assessment. Better if alignment is a new concept for participants and needs its own dedicated moment. Worse if participants already grasp alignment through Exercise 5's live discussion — in that case the audit is higher-energy and covers more of the learning science outcomes. Takes 10 min less than the audit, which can absorb overruns elsewhere.

**Adapted instructions:**
Teams return to their work from Exercises 4 and 5. In their team breakout room (5 min), they talk through three questions about their activity design:

- **Outcome:** Which outcome does this activity address?
- **Activity:** What do learners actually do?
- **Evidence:** If a learner completed this activity, what would you actually see or hear that tells you they achieved the outcome?

The team's job is to spot misalignment — does the activity actually practise the outcome, or does it practise something adjacent? Does the "evidence" describe something observable, or is it vague ("learners understood the concept")?

Quick whole-group check (3 min): facilitator asks "Which teams found a misalignment?" Brief discussion of what the most common mismatches were.

**Time:** 10 min (2 min setup / 5 min team discussion in breakout / 3 min whole-group share)

**Outcomes addressed:** L5.2 (backward design), L5.5 (diagnose misalignment), L9.1 (distinguish what assessment looks like — lightly), L9.2 (formative assessment concept — lightly)

### Alternative C: Practice & Feedback Swap

**Original activity:** Activity 9: Practice and Feedback Plan (Lesson 8)

**Could replace:** Exercise 6 (Learner Experience Audit)

**Grouping:** Own team — they're adding a feedback plan to the activity they began designing in Exercise 5.

**Tradeoff:** You lose the learning-science diagnostic, but gain deeper engagement with feedback design — specifically, how to make feedback specific, timely, and actionable. This is a natural extension of Exercise 5's design work and makes the feedback principles explicit. Takes 5 min less than the audit. Better if the audience struggles with giving useful feedback (common among researchers). Worse if the group needs more grounding in learning science principles.

**Adapted instructions:**
Teams return to their breakout rooms and revisit the activity they designed in Exercise 5. Together (5 min), they add a feedback plan: at what point during the activity would learners receive feedback? From whom? What would the feedback focus on? How would learners use it to improve?

Cross-check (5 min): one member from each team joins a cross-team breakout room and describes their feedback plan. The others check: Is the feedback specific enough to act on? Does it arrive while revision is still possible? Could a learner improve their work based on this feedback?

Brief debrief (2 min): facilitator reinforces the distinction between feedback that helps ("your conclusion doesn't follow from the data — what's the link?") and feedback that doesn't ("good job" / "needs work").

**Time:** 15 min (3 min setup / 5 min team discussion in breakout / 5 min cross-team check in breakout / 2 min debrief)

**Outcomes addressed:** L8.1 (design practice opportunities), L8.2 (plan feedback processes), L8.3 (specific, timely, actionable feedback), L8.4 (build iteration cycles), L5.5 (diagnose misalignment — lightly, through checking whether feedback connects to outcomes)
