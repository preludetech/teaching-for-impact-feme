  • The system your training lives in
  • Your learners and their realities

Training lives in a system

Training is not a standalone event. It is a deliberate intervention within a broader system of people, resources, constraints, and relationships.

Not "What should I teach?"
but "What change is possible in this system — and how can training contribute?"

5-minute framing. Key message: before you design content, understand the conditions that determine whether your training leads to change. Training that ignores its context can be perfectly designed and still have zero lasting impact.

What to look for in your system

  • Actors — who affects whether the training succeeds? (Not just who is in the room)
  • Resources — what does the training depend on? What is fragile?
  • Constraints — what limits what is possible?
  • Relationships — who depends on whom? What flows where?

Push beyond the obvious — the actors who most affect long-term impact are often further out.

Give a quick example: "If you're training community health workers, the system includes not just the trainees and trainers, but their managers, the clinics they work in, the data systems they report to, the communities they serve. A manager who won't release staff for follow-up practice can undo everything you achieve in the training room."

Exercise 1

System Map

Map the system around your team's training.

  • Who are the actors?
  • What resources do you depend on?
  • What constraints shape what is possible?
  • What relationships connect these elements?

Push beyond the obvious — who affects the training after learners leave the room?

Own-team breakout rooms. One person can sketch or take notes on their screen, but the point is the conversation. Encourage teams to push past the "trainer + learner + funder" triangle and think about downstream actors. 15 minutes in breakout. Setup: 2 min. Total: 25 min.

From systems to change

You've mapped the system your training lives in.

Now: how does your training lead to change within that system?

5-minute framing transition. This connects the system map (what exists) to the theory of change (what you intend). Key message: understanding a system doesn't automatically tell you what change is possible — you need to make that logic explicit.

Theory of Change

A simple, honest account of the logic connecting what you do to the impact you hope for.

Impact ← Outcomes ← Outputs ← Activities ← Inputs

Start from the right: what impact do you hope to contribute to?
Then work backwards.

Explain the chain briefly. The key move is starting from impact and working backwards — this keeps teams honest. It's easy to list activities you enjoy delivering, but harder to explain how they lead to real-world change. Give the NGO health worker example from lesson 2 if helpful: training on data collection → health workers routinely collect reliable data → better resource allocation. The hidden assumption was phone signal and electricity at clinics.

The question at every link

What has to be true for this step to lead to the next?

Those are your assumptions. Write them down.

Each assumption is a potential point of failure — and a potential design decision.

Give a concrete example: "If your chain says 'participants learn data analysis → they apply those skills at work', you're assuming they have access to data, their managers support new approaches, and they have time to practise. Each of those could break the chain." This sets up Exercise 2's assumption-hunting phase.

  • What could break here?

From systems to people

You've mapped your system and made your logic explicit.

Now: who are the people your training is actually for?

Not the registration-form version — the human version.

5-minute framing. Key message: system maps and theories of change are about the bigger picture, but there is one part of the system that deserves far more attention than a box on a diagram — the people in the room. Transition to learner-centred thinking.

Know your learners

Training works when it aligns with how people actually live, work, and learn.

  • Access — devices, connectivity, spaces
  • Time — competing demands, release from work
  • Language — does the training match?
  • Culture & power — who speaks? Who stays silent?
  • Motivation — why are they in the room?
Brief framing: the most important design information is rarely on a registration form. Emphasise that learners always bring something — professional experience, local knowledge, informal skills. A needs assessment that only maps deficits misses the assets the training can build on.

  2. What do they already know or do that your training can build on?
  3. What is the single biggest barrier to their participation or success?
  4. What does "success" look like from their perspective?
Put all four prompts on screen before sending people to breakout rooms. Give 2 minutes of silent thinking time first. Then cross-team breakout rooms (reuse the same groupings from Exercise 2 Phase 2 — saves a reshuffle and groups are warmed up). Each person takes about 2 min to describe their learners, focusing on prompts 3 and 4.

How people actually learn

Training is not effective simply because content is delivered. Learning depends on what learners do with that content.

From: "How do I present my content clearly?"
To: "How do people actually learn — and how do I design for that?"

10-minute mini-lecture covering four key principles. This provides the conceptual foundation for the Learner Experience Audit (Exercise 6) later. Keep it brisk — participants will apply these ideas hands-on soon. Start by debunking learning styles: the idea is intuitive, widely repeated, and wrong. The method should match the material and the learning outcome, not an individual's self-reported style.

Cognitive load

Working memory is limited. When too much arrives at once, learners cannot process any of it well.

But not all struggle is bad:

  • Intrinsic load — the effort of engaging with the material itself. This is the point. Do not remove it.
  • Extraneous load — effort caused by poor design (confusing instructions, cluttered slides, unclear tasks). This adds nothing.

Make sure learners struggle with the material, not with your instructions.

Give the example from lesson 4: a trainer asks participants to clean a dataset, identify patterns, and present findings — all in one instruction. Learners struggle to understand what to do first, not with the analysis. Breaking it into three stages with clear instructions fixes the extraneous load while keeping the intrinsic challenge.

Retrieval and reinforcement

Retrieval: learning is stronger when people recall information from memory rather than re-read it.

Recognition ("I've seen this") is not the same as recall ("I can explain this and use it").

Reinforcement: a single exposure is usually not enough. Revisit key ideas in new ways — different problems, different contexts.

Quick practical examples: short recall questions between sections, pair discussions where learners explain ideas to each other, activities that build on knowledge from a previous session without re-presenting it. The point is to build in moments where learners actively pull knowledge out, not just take it in.

Social learning

Discussion, collaboration, and shared problem-solving are not just engagement strategies — they are learning mechanisms.

  • Explaining your thinking forces you to organise and articulate ideas
  • Hearing different perspectives forces you to reconcile them with your own

Design social learning intentionally — it will not happen on its own.

Practical implications: pair discussions with specific prompts, small-group tasks with clear outputs, peer review with structured questions. This principle will be visible throughout the rest of the session — the peer swap in Exercise 5 and the cross-team audit in Exercise 6 both rely on social learning. Point this out so participants recognise it when it happens.

From principles to practice

These four ideas — cognitive load, retrieval, reinforcement, social learning — are not separate add-ons.

They are lenses for every design decision you make from here on.

Next: defining what your training is actually trying to achieve.

Brief transition. Connect the learning science to what comes next: the outcomes they write should be things learners DO (retrieval, application), not things they passively receive. The activities they design should manage cognitive load and use social learning intentionally.

Learning outcomes

A learning outcome describes what a learner will be able to do, not what the training will cover.

✗ Vague "Understand climate data"
✓ Strong "Interpret a local dataset to identify two practical risks and explain them to a non-specialist audience"
5-minute framing built into the exercise time. Show 2–3 examples of vague vs strong outcomes. Strong outcomes are: observable (you can see evidence), action-oriented (use a verb), and relevant (connected to what people need to do after training). The key test: "Could I observe a learner achieving this?"

Three qualities of strong outcomes

  • Observable — you can see or hear evidence of it
  • Action-oriented — uses a verb: interpret, design, explain, analyse
  • Relevant — connected to what learners need to do in the real world

The test: Could you observe a learner achieving this — and how would you know?

Mention Bloom's taxonomy briefly as a source for action verbs if teams get stuck, but don't dwell on it. The point is practical: write outcomes that describe visible performance, not internal mental states.

  • Does it use an action verb?
  • Is it realistic for your learners?

Exercise 4: Learning Outcomes

Phase 2 — Cross-team critique

Cross-team breakout (8 min)

Read out your team's outcomes. Others check:

  1. Could you observe a learner achieving this — how would you know?
  2. Is any outcome actually a topic description disguised as an outcome?

Note revisions.

Reshuffle breakout rooms (cross-team). Each person reads their outcomes aloud. Reviewers check for observability and disguised topic descriptions. 8 min in breakout. Then 3 min whole-group debrief: ask for 1–2 "before and after" examples — an outcome that improved through critique. Use these to reinforce the observable/action-oriented principle. Total: 25 min.

From outcomes to activities

You now have outcomes that describe what learners will do.

Next question: what activity will get them there?

Activities are where learning happens — not in your slides, not in your explanations, but in the moments where learners discuss, analyse, build, practise, and apply.

5-minute framing. Key message from lesson 5: content should be driven by activities, not topics. Every activity starts from a learning outcome. If a piece of content doesn't directly help learners succeed in an activity, it doesn't belong — no matter how interesting it seems. This connects back to cognitive load: unnecessary content is extraneous load by definition.

Exercise 5

Design One Activity

Phase 1 — Design (25 min, own team)

Select one learning outcome from Exercise 4. Design a 15–20 minute activity that helps learners achieve it.

This is the integrative exercise — teams apply everything from the morning. Own-team breakout rooms for 25 min. One team member should take notes or type up the design so they can screen-share in Phase 2. If teams don't get through all six components, prioritise Objective, Instructions, and Output — these are enough for a useful peer swap.

The six components

Talk through each one for your activity:

  1. Objective — which outcome does this address?
  2. Instructions — step-by-step, clear enough for another facilitator to run
  3. Materials — what do learners need?
  4. Time — how long, broken into stages
  5. Output — what do learners produce? (something concrete)
  6. Participation — how is everyone involved, not just the confident ones?
Keep this slide visible or remind teams to refer back to it. The six components come from lesson 7. If teams are stuck, suggest they start with: "What would a learner need to DO to demonstrate this outcome?" — that action becomes the core of the activity. Priority if time is short: Objective, Instructions, Output.

Exercise 5: Peer Swap

Phase 2 — Cross-team review

Cross-team breakout (12 min)

One team presents their activity. The others review:

  • Could you actually run this activity from these instructions?
  • Does the output genuinely show whether learners achieved the outcome?
  • Where might learners get stuck, and is there support built in?

Then swap — the other team presents.

Reshuffle breakout rooms so each has representatives from two different teams. One team presents, others review with the three questions. Then swap. Each side gives verbal feedback. 12 min in breakout. Then 5 min whole-group harvest: "What was the most useful feedback you received — something that changed your design?" Collect 3–4 examples. Reinforce that peer feedback is a learning mechanism. Total exercise time: 45 min.

Putting on a learner's hat

You've just designed an activity. Now let's look at design through a different lens — the learner's experience.

Where does design help learning? Where does it get in the way?

Brief transition to Exercise 6. This exercise applies the learning science from the mini-lecture. Participants have just designed their own activity, so the question "which of these problems do you recognise in your own designs?" will land with real force.

Exercise 6

Learner Experience Audit

Here is a flawed training segment. Your job: find the problems.

Cross-team breakout (10 min) — audit against three questions:

  1. Where would learners feel overwhelmed — is that the material's fault or the design's fault?
  2. Where are learners passive for more than 10 minutes — what could they do instead?
  3. Where could learners learn from each other — and what structure would make that work?
Facilitator prep: you should have created a 1-page flawed training segment before the workshop. A 15–20 min session plan with realistic problems: long passive lecture block, vague instructions, no learner output, missed social learning opportunities, cognitive overload. Base it on a plausible domain (e.g. data literacy). Screen-share it now. Cross-team breakout rooms of 3–4 people. 10 min to audit. This can be skipped if behind schedule. If cutting, briefly mention the three audit questions verbally and ask teams to apply them to their own Exercise 5 designs.
