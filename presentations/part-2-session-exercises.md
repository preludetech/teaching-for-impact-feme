# Part 2 Workshop: Session Plan

## Summary

- **Total session time:** 4 hours (240 minutes), including one 20-minute break
- **Content time:** ~3 hours 10 minutes
- **Exercises:** 6 recommended (4 core + 2 stretch) + 3 alternatives available
- **If running long:** Drop Exercise 6 (OER Lifecycle Planning) first to reclaim 20 min. If still pressed, drop Exercise 5 (Peer Usability Test) to reclaim another 25 min. Core exercises 1–4 can stand alone as a complete arc.
- **Format:** Remote video call with breakout rooms. No shared canvas or collaborative tools — exercises rely on conversation and screen-sharing only
- **Participants:** Teams developing their own training, continuing from Part 1. Each team has a training design (outcomes, activities, learner analysis) from the Part 1 workshop.
- **Team size:** 3–5 people per team. If a team has only 1–2 members, pair them with another small team for own-team exercises. If a team has 6+, split into sub-groups for breakout work and reunite for whole-group phases.
- **Facilitator prep required:** Before the workshop, select 2 real OER examples relevant to the group's general domain. These are used in Exercise 2. Choose resources with visible licenses and clear strengths/weaknesses — one that's mostly good but has hidden assumptions, and one that looks useful but has licensing or context-fit problems.
- **Prerequisite:** Teams should bring their training design from Part 1 — specifically their Activity 13 snapshot and at least one Activity 8 activity design. Remind them before the workshop to have these accessible on screen.

## Recommended exercises

### Exercise 1: Hidden Assumptions Swap — core

**Original activity:** Activity 14: OER Workflow, Section 0 (Context) and Section 2 (Hidden Assumptions) — applied through the lens of Lesson 1: Designing for Openness

**Grouping:** Own team for Phase 1 (audit), then cross-team mix for Phase 2 (swap) — teams know their own materials but outsiders are better at spotting what's been left unstated.

**Adapted instructions:**
Phase 1 — Audit your own material (10 min, own team breakout): Each team selects one activity or resource from their Part 1 training design (Activity 8 or any material they've been developing). They discuss four questions:

1. Could someone run this activity without you explaining anything?
2. What prior knowledge does it assume — that you've never written down?
3. What tools, access, or infrastructure does it depend on?
4. What examples or terminology would not make sense to learners in a different context?

One person takes notes. The goal is to surface at least 3 hidden assumptions.

Phase 2 — Cross-team swap (8 min, cross-team breakout): Breakout rooms are reshuffled so each room has members from different teams. Each person briefly describes their team's material and the assumptions they found. The others respond with: "Here's one more assumption I'd add" — outsiders almost always spot things the team has stopped seeing. Each person notes the new assumptions raised by others.

Whole-group debrief (4 min): Facilitator asks 2–3 people to name the assumption that surprised them most — the one their team had completely overlooked. Discussion: were these assumptions about learners, tools, or context? Facilitator connects this to the core Part 2 idea: if you want your materials to have a life beyond your own delivery, hidden assumptions are the first thing to address.

**Time:** 25 min (3 min setup / 10 min own-team audit in breakout / 8 min cross-team swap in breakout / 4 min whole-group debrief)

**Reason for inclusion:** This is the foundational mindset shift for Part 2. Participants move from "this works when I deliver it" to "could this work without me?" It's concrete — they audit real materials, not hypothetical ones — and the cross-team swap reliably surfaces assumptions that teams can no longer see. This exercise sets up everything that follows: evaluation, adaptation, and sharing all depend on understanding what's implicit in your materials.

**Outcomes addressed:** L1.1 (explain how openness supports reuse — through experiencing why assumptions block it), L1.3 (identify features of reusable materials — by contrast with what's currently missing), L1.4 (recognise hidden assumptions), L4.2 (identify assumptions in resources — applied to own work)

### Exercise 2: OER Evaluation — core

**Original activity:** Activity 14: OER Workflow, Section 1 (Find and Evaluate Resources) — applied through Lesson 2: Finding and Evaluating Existing OER

**Grouping:** Own team — they evaluate resources against their own training's needs, so the criteria are grounded in their specific context.

**Facilitator prep:** Before the workshop, select 2 real OER examples. Choose resources that are:
- Relevant to the group's general domain (but not any specific team's topic)
- Openly licensed (one CC BY, one with a more restrictive license like CC BY-NC-SA)
- Varied in quality: one with clear instructions but context-specific assumptions, one with good content but poor documentation

Screen-share both briefly (2 min each) before sending teams to breakout rooms.

**Adapted instructions:**
Facilitator screen-shares both OER examples, walking through what each one is and where it comes from. Then teams go to breakout rooms (15 min). Each team picks one of the two resources and evaluates it using five criteria:

1. **Alignment** — Does this support learning outcomes similar to yours? Is the level appropriate?
2. **Clarity** — Could your learners follow this without heavy explanation?
3. **Context fit** — Are the examples meaningful? Does it assume tools or access your learners don't have?
4. **Adaptability** — Could you realistically modify this? Is it in an editable format?
5. **Licensing** — What does the license allow you to do? (They may not know yet — that's fine, it sets up Exercise 3.)

Teams reach a verdict: reuse as is, adapt, or don't use. They note one strength and one risk.

Whole-group share (5 min): Facilitator asks teams who evaluated the same resource to compare verdicts. Did they agree? Where did they diverge? Discussion focuses on how context changes what "fit" means — the same resource can be a good fit for one team and a poor fit for another.

**Time:** 25 min (3 min setup + facilitator walkthrough / 15 min team evaluation in breakout / 5 min whole-group share / 2 min debrief)

**Reason for inclusion:** Evaluation is the most practical skill in Part 2 — it's what participants will do every time they find a resource online. Using real OER makes the exercise concrete rather than theoretical. Having different teams reach different verdicts on the same resource powerfully demonstrates that evaluation is context-dependent.

**Outcomes addressed:** L2.2 (evaluate using five criteria), L2.3 (recognise risks), L2.4 (make reuse/adapt/create decisions), L2.5 (select resources for context), L1.2 (decide when to reuse, adapt, or create)

### Exercise 3: License Scenarios — core

**Original activity:** Activity 14: OER Workflow, Section 1 (licensing evaluation) — applied through Lesson 3: What Makes Resources Open?

**Grouping:** Cross-team mix — licensing is not team-specific, and discussing scenarios with people from different projects surfaces a wider range of practical situations.

**Adapted instructions:**
The mini-lecture (10 min before this exercise) covers the CC license elements (BY, SA, NC, ND) with a simple visual. Keep it brief — the exercise is where understanding happens.

Facilitator reads out 4 scenarios, one at a time. After each scenario, cross-team breakout rooms (3–4 people) discuss for 2 minutes, then return to the main room for a quick answer and explanation. Scenarios build in complexity:

1. **Straightforward reuse:** You find a CC BY slide deck on data literacy. You want to translate it into another language and use it in your workshop. Can you? What must you do?
2. **ND restriction:** You find a CC BY-ND video tutorial that's almost perfect, but 10 minutes too long for your session. Can you edit it down? What are your options?
3. **Combining licenses:** You want to merge two resources into one handout — one is CC BY, the other is CC BY-SA. Can you combine them? What license goes on the result?
4. **NC ambiguity:** Your training is free for participants but funded by a grant. You want to use a CC BY-NC resource. Is your use "commercial"? How would you decide?

After all 4 scenarios, facilitator asks: "Which scenario made you most uncertain?" Brief whole-group discussion (3 min) to reinforce that licensing doesn't require perfect answers — it requires knowing what questions to ask.

**Time:** 20 min (2 min setup / 4 × 2 min breakout + 1 min debrief per scenario = 12 min / 3 min whole-group discussion / 3 min buffer)

**Reason for inclusion:** Licensing is essential knowledge that most researchers have never engaged with. Scenarios make it concrete and memorable rather than abstract. The escalating complexity builds confidence — participants start with an easy win and end grappling with genuine ambiguity. Cross-team grouping keeps energy high and ensures no team dominates the conversation. Placing this after the OER Evaluation exercise means participants already have licensing questions from Exercise 2 that this exercise helps answer.

**Outcomes addressed:** L3.1 (explain how open licenses enable reuse — through experiencing what each license allows), L3.2 (distinguish CC license types), L3.4 (apply attribution — scenario 1), L3.5 (identify risks combining licenses — scenarios 3 and 4)

### Exercise 4: Adaptation Workshop — core

**Original activity:** Activity 14: OER Workflow, Section 3 (Adaptation and Localisation Log) — applied through Lesson 4: Adapting, Remixing, and Localising OER

**Grouping:** Own team for Phase 1 (adaptation planning), then cross-team pairs for Phase 2 (review) — teams adapt for their own context, then get feedback on whether their adaptation decisions are clear and well-reasoned.

**Adapted instructions:**
This is the most hands-on exercise in the workshop. Teams apply what they've learned about assumptions, evaluation, and licensing to actually plan an adaptation.

Phase 1 — Plan the adaptation (20 min, own team breakout): Each team selects one resource to adapt. This can be:
- One of the OER from Exercise 2
- A resource they've already been considering for their training
- One of their own Part 1 materials that needs adaptation for a different context

Teams work through a structured conversation:

1. **Surface assumptions** — What does this resource assume about learners, tools, and context? (Build on Exercise 1 skills.)
2. **Identify mismatches** — Compare assumptions against your learners and context. Where are the gaps?
3. **Categorise changes** — For each mismatch, is the adaptation surface-level (wording, examples, format), structural (sequence, activities, emphasis), or contextual (goals, delivery conditions)?
4. **Decide: keep, modify, or remove** — For each element, make a concrete decision. If modifying, describe the change.
5. **Check licensing** — Does the license allow the adaptations you're planning? (Apply Exercise 3 knowledge.)

One team member documents the decisions.

Phase 2 — Cross-team review (8 min, cross-team breakout): Breakout rooms are reshuffled. One person from each team presents their adaptation plan: what the resource is, what they're changing, and why. The reviewer asks: "Is there an assumption you've kept that you shouldn't have?" and "Is any of your adaptation more effort than creating something new would be?"

Whole-group harvest (4 min): Facilitator asks: "Who discovered during this exercise that adaptation was more work than expected — and decided to create instead?" Discussion: the reuse-adapt-create decision is not fixed. It changes as you understand the resource better.

**Time:** 35 min (3 min setup / 20 min own-team adaptation planning in breakout / 8 min cross-team review in breakout / 4 min whole-group harvest)

**Reason for inclusion:** This is the integrative exercise for the first half of Part 2 — it pulls together assumption awareness (Exercise 1), evaluation criteria (Exercise 2), and licensing knowledge (Exercise 3) into a single practical task. Teams produce a concrete adaptation plan they can use after the workshop. The cross-team review catches blind spots and reinforces that adaptation decisions should be explicit and documented.

**Outcomes addressed:** L4.1 (adapt OER to align with context), L4.2 (identify and modify assumptions), L4.3 (distinguish surface-level and structural adaptation), L4.4 (decide what to change, retain, or remove), L4.5 (document adaptations — through the structured conversation), L2.4 (reuse/adapt/create decisions — reinforced through the "create instead?" question)

### Exercise 5: Peer Usability Test — stretch

**Original activity:** Activity 14: OER Workflow, Section 4 (Prepare for Sharing) — applied through Lesson 5: Sharing and Publishing OER

**Grouping:** Cross-team pairs — the entire point is that someone unfamiliar with your materials tries to use them. Pair teams that haven't worked together yet in the workshop.

**Adapted instructions:**
This exercise tests the core question of Lesson 5: can your materials be used without you?

Phase 1 — Prepare (3 min, own team): Each team selects one piece of their training material — an activity design, a slide deck, a handout, anything concrete. They will share it with another team. No pre-explanation allowed — the material must speak for itself.

Phase 2 — Usability test (12 min, cross-team breakout): Two teams are paired in a breakout room. Team A screen-shares their material. Team B has 5 minutes to read/view it and answer three questions:
- What is this material for? Who is it aimed at?
- Could you run or use this without talking to the person who made it?
- Where would you get stuck or confused?

Team A listens silently — no clarifying or defending. Then Team B gives verbal feedback: what was clear, what was missing, and one specific suggestion. Swap roles and repeat.

Phase 3 — Whole-group debrief (5 min): Facilitator asks: "What was the most common thing that was missing?" Collect answers — they almost always cluster around: context (who is this for?), instructions (what do learners actually do?), and assumptions (what do you need to know beforehand?). Facilitator connects this to documentation: the things your testers couldn't find are exactly what you need to document before sharing.

**Time:** 25 min (3 min setup + prep / 12 min cross-team usability test in breakout / 5 min whole-group debrief / 5 min buffer)

**Reason for inclusion:** This is the most experiential exercise in the workshop. Instead of talking about what makes materials shareable, teams experience the gap between "clear to me" and "clear to someone else." The silent-listening rule prevents teams from papering over usability problems with verbal explanations — which is exactly what happens in real life when materials aren't self-explanatory. This exercise reliably produces "aha" moments.

**Outcomes addressed:** L5.1 (prepare OER for use without explanation), L5.3 (ensure materials are understandable by others), L5.4 (document to support reuse — through identifying what's missing), L5.5 (decide when a resource is ready to share — through discovering it isn't yet), L1.3 (identify features of reusable materials — through contrast)

**If cut:** Lessons 5 outcomes lose their dedicated exercise. L5.1 and L5.3 are partially covered by Exercise 1 (hidden assumptions are one reason materials aren't self-explanatory). L5.4 and L5.5 would need to be addressed in the closing reflection by asking teams to identify what documentation their materials need. The experiential "aha" moment is lost entirely — this is the main cost of cutting.

### Exercise 6: OER Lifecycle Planning — stretch

**Original activity:** Activity 14: OER Workflow, Section 5 (Sustainability and Improvement) — applied through Lesson 6: Sustaining and Improving OER

**Grouping:** Own team — sustainability decisions are specific to each team's capacity, context, and goals.

**Adapted instructions:**
This is a reflective, lower-energy exercise that helps teams think beyond initial creation. It works well as a wind-down before the closing.

Teams go to breakout rooms (12 min) and discuss three questions:

1. **Maintenance level** — After you share your materials, how will you maintain them? Choose one:
   - Active (regular updates after each use)
   - Occasional (update when something breaks or changes significantly)
   - Archive (share as-is, no plans to update)
   There is no wrong answer — the wrong answer is not deciding.

2. **Feedback plan** — What feedback would actually help you improve your materials? From whom? When? Be specific — "feedback from users" is not a plan. "Ask facilitators after each delivery whether any instructions were unclear" is a plan.

3. **One versioning decision** — How will you track changes? Teams don't need a full versioning system — they need one concrete decision. For example: "We'll use v1.0/v1.1 naming and keep a changelog in the README" or "We'll use dates: 2026-03 edition."

Each team writes down their three decisions. These feed directly into Activity 14 Section 5 in the workbook.

Whole-group share (5 min): Facilitator asks one team from each maintenance level (active, occasional, archive) to briefly explain their choice and why it's realistic for them. Discussion: sustainability is not about doing the most — it's about matching your maintenance to your capacity. Materials that are honestly archived are better than materials that are theoretically maintained but actually abandoned.

**Time:** 20 min (3 min setup / 12 min own-team discussion in breakout / 5 min whole-group share)

**Reason for inclusion:** Most OER conversations focus on creation and sharing, not on what happens afterwards. This exercise forces teams to make explicit sustainability decisions before the workshop ends — while the energy of the session is still present. The "one versioning decision" prompt is deliberately modest: it gives teams a concrete starting point rather than an overwhelming system to design. Placing this as the final exercise creates a natural bridge to the closing reflection.

**Outcomes addressed:** L6.1 (understand OER lifecycle), L6.2 (identify realistic maintenance approaches), L6.3 (collect and use feedback — through designing a feedback plan), L6.4 (decide when to update, maintain, or archive), L6.5 (ensure materials remain usable — through the sustainability commitment)

**If cut:** Lesson 6 outcomes are entirely uncovered by exercises. The facilitator can partially address this in the closing reflection by asking: "What will happen to your materials six months from now?" This surfaces the sustainability question without a dedicated exercise. The workbook covers this lesson thoroughly, so it's a reasonable gap for a 4-hour session focused on breadth.

## Alternative exercises

### Alternative A: Reuse-Adapt-Create Decision Matrix

**Original activity:** Activity 14: OER Workflow, Section 2 (Design Decisions) — applied through Lessons 1 and 2

**Could replace:** Exercise 2 (OER Evaluation)

**Grouping:** Own team — they're making decisions about their own training materials.

**Tradeoff:** You lose the shared OER evaluation experience (seeing how different teams evaluate the same resource differently) and the hands-on practice with the five evaluation criteria. You gain a more personalised exercise where teams make concrete reuse/adapt/create decisions about their own materials — producing an output they can use immediately. Better if teams already have candidate resources identified before the workshop. Worse if teams are new to OER and need to see examples first. Takes the same time (25 min).

**Adapted instructions:**
Teams go to breakout rooms (15 min). They list 3–5 components of their training (activities, slides, handouts, datasets, etc.). For each component, they quickly decide: reuse an existing resource, adapt one, or create from scratch. They note the reasoning — what makes reuse possible or impossible for each component?

Cross-team share (5 min): One person from each team joins a cross-team breakout and describes their most surprising decision — the component where they chose differently than they expected to.

Whole-group debrief (3 min): Facilitator asks: "Who found that they could reuse more than they thought? Who found they need to create more than they expected?" Discussion: most trainers underestimate how much they can reuse and overestimate how unique their context is.

**Time:** 25 min (2 min setup / 15 min team discussion in breakout / 5 min cross-team share / 3 min debrief)

**Outcomes addressed:** L1.2 (decide when to reuse, adapt, or create), L2.2 (evaluate for fit — applied to own materials), L2.4 (make explicit reuse/adapt/create decisions), L2.5 (select resources for context)

### Alternative B: License Your Own Work

**Original activity:** Activity 14: OER Workflow, Section 4 (licensing decision) — applied through Lesson 3

**Could replace:** Exercise 3 (License Scenarios)

**Grouping:** Own team first (decision), then cross-team discussion (comparison) — licensing decisions are team-specific but comparing choices with other teams reveals different priorities and trade-offs.

**Tradeoff:** You lose the diagnostic skill of reading and interpreting others' licenses (the scenario-based practice). You gain a personally relevant decision about how to license your own materials — something teams can act on immediately. Better if participants already understand basic CC license types (e.g., from pre-reading) and need to apply that knowledge. Worse if licensing is entirely new — in that case, the scenarios build understanding more effectively. The mini-lecture on licensing essentials still runs before either exercise.

**Adapted instructions:**
Teams go to breakout rooms (8 min). They answer three questions:
1. What do you want others to be able to do with your materials? (Reuse, adapt, share, use commercially?)
2. What do you want to prevent? (Commercial use, modifications, redistribution without attribution?)
3. Based on your answers, which CC license fits?

Cross-team discussion (5 min): Breakout rooms reshuffled. Each person shares their team's license choice and reasoning. Others ask: "Have you considered what happens if someone wants to combine your materials with a ShareAlike resource?"

Whole-group debrief (3 min): Facilitator asks for the range of choices across teams. Discussion: different goals lead to different licenses, and that's fine — the important thing is making a deliberate choice rather than defaulting to "all rights reserved" by not choosing.

**Time:** 20 min (4 min setup / 8 min team discussion in breakout / 5 min cross-team discussion in breakout / 3 min debrief)

**Outcomes addressed:** L3.1 (explain how open licenses enable reuse), L3.2 (distinguish CC license types — through making a choice), L3.3 (make informed licensing decisions for your own materials), L3.5 (identify combination risks — through the cross-team question)

### Alternative C: Documentation Sprint

**Original activity:** Activity 14: OER Workflow, Section 4 (Prepare for Sharing) — applied through Lesson 5

**Could replace:** Exercise 5 (Peer Usability Test)

**Grouping:** Own team — they're documenting their own materials.

**Tradeoff:** You lose the experiential "aha" moment of watching someone else fail to use your materials. You gain actual documentation — teams leave the workshop with one resource that has real written context, instructions, and assumptions. More productive in terms of tangible output. Less memorable as a learning experience. Better if time is tight (takes 5 min less) or if teams' materials are too early-stage for a usability test. Worse if teams already have decent documentation and need the outside perspective more than the writing time.

**Adapted instructions:**
Teams go to breakout rooms (15 min). They select one resource or activity and write documentation for it, covering:
- **Purpose:** What is this material for?
- **Audience:** Who is it designed for?
- **Instructions:** How should someone use it?
- **Assumptions:** What does a user need to know or have access to?
- **License:** How is this licensed?

One person writes while the team dictates. Focus on clarity over completeness — better to document three things well than five things vaguely.

Quick cross-team check (3 min): One person from each team joins a cross-team breakout and reads their documentation aloud. The others say what's clear and what's missing in one sentence each.

**Time:** 20 min (2 min setup / 15 min team documentation in breakout / 3 min cross-team check)

**Outcomes addressed:** L5.1 (prepare OER for use without explanation), L5.3 (ensure materials are understandable), L5.4 (document to support reuse — directly), L5.5 (decide when ready to share)

## Opening and closing details

### Opening warm-up (0:00–0:15)

**Format:** Cross-team breakout then whole-group share

**Grouping:** Cross-team mix — people meet someone from another team and start thinking about reuse through their own experience.

Participants go to cross-team breakout rooms (3–4 people from different teams). Each person has 2 minutes to describe a time they tried to use someone else's training material, slide deck, lesson plan, or educational resource. The prompt:

> "What happened when you tried to use it? What worked, and what was missing?"

If someone hasn't reused a resource, they can describe a time they handed their own materials to someone else and it didn't go as expected.

After 6 minutes of breakout conversation, everyone returns to the main room. The facilitator asks 3–4 people to share what was missing. Responses almost always cluster around: unclear instructions, missing context, assumed knowledge, wrong format, or licensing confusion. The facilitator captures these themes verbally — they become reference points throughout the workshop.

Close the warm-up with a 2-minute framing of the workshop arc: "In Part 1, you designed training that works for your learners. In Part 2, you're making that training work beyond you — so others can find it, understand it, adapt it, and improve it. Today we'll work through the full arc: from understanding what makes materials reusable, to evaluating and adapting resources, to preparing your own work for sharing. Everything we do today, you'll do with your own training in mind."

### Closing reflection (3:45–4:00)

**Format:** Whole-group share (no breakout — time is short and communal energy matters at the close)

Facilitator asks each team to answer two questions aloud:

1. "What is one thing about your training materials that you now see differently?"
2. "What is the first concrete thing you will do after this workshop to make your materials more open or reusable?"

Go round quickly — one answer per team for each question. The first question surfaces mindset shifts; the second produces commitments.

Close by pointing participants to the workbook for deeper engagement — especially Activity 14 (OER Workflow), which structures the full process they've sampled today. End with: "The goal today was not to finish your OER plan — it was to see the full picture and build the habits that make openness practical. The workbook is where you do the detailed work. The most important thing you take away today is the instinct to ask: could someone else use this without me?"

Facilitator notes down the commitments — these are valuable for follow-up and for assessing whether the workshop shifted practice.
