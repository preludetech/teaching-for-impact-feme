    8. Assessment and Evaluation

    🎯 Learning Outcomes 📍 Guiding Questions
    • Distinguish between assessment (learning-focused) and evaluation (impact-focused)
    • Design formative and summative assessments aligned with learning outcomes
    • Define clear, observable indicators of success
    • Select appropriate and feasible methods for collecting evidence of learning and impact
    • Evaluate evidence to distinguish meaningful signal from noise
    • How will you know if learning has occurred?
    • What is the difference between assessing learning and evaluating impact in this context?
    • Are your assessments aligned with your intended outcomes?
    • What indicators best reflect meaningful success?
    • What evidence will you collect, and how will it help you improve your training?

    Why this matters¶

    In the previous section, you designed opportunities for practice, feedback, and iteration within your learning activities.

    This section focuses on how you recognise learning and understand impact as part of your overall training design.

    Assessment should not be something added only at the end. It is part of how you design outcomes, activities, and feedback so that learning is visible.

    Training is often evaluated using attendance or satisfaction. These can be useful, but they do not fully tell you:

    • whether learning happened
    • whether participants can apply what they learned
    • whether the training made a meaningful difference

    Effective training uses assessment to make learning visible and evaluation to better understand impact over time.

    This section helps you move from...

    “Did the training run?”
    to:
    “How do I know learning happened, and what changed as a result?”

    Core concepts¶

    Assessment and evaluation

    Assessment focuses on what learners can do, while evaluation focuses on whether the training contributed to the intended outcomes or broader change.

    Assessment
    Assessment focuses on what learners know, produce, decide, or do. It should align with the outcomes you defined earlier.

    Evaluation
    Evaluation looks beyond the learning activity itself to understand change over time. It asks whether the training led to useful change in practice, confidence, behaviour, or other intended outcomes.

    Indicators, evidence, and signal
    Indicators are observable signs of learning or change. Evidence is what you collect to judge those indicators. Useful evidence provides signal, not just noise.

    Practical guidance¶

    Step 1 — Define what success looks like¶

    Start with your outcomes and ask:

    • What would learners be able to do if learning is successful?
    • What would meaningful change look like afterwards?

    Define two or three clear, observable indicators.

    Step 2 — Design assessment aligned with outcomes and activities¶

    For each outcome, decide how learners will demonstrate it.

    Examples might include:

    • completing a task
    • producing an output
    • making and justifying a decision
    • applying a method in context

    Step 3 — Use formative and summative assessment¶

    Include:

    • formative assessment during learning to support improvement
    • summative assessment at the end to judge achievement

    Use formative approaches wherever possible.

    Step 4 — Collect useful and feasible evidence¶

    Use methods such as:

    • observation
    • outputs or artefacts
    • short reflections
    • brief follow-up questions
    • peer or facilitator review

    Choose methods you can realistically apply in your context.

    Step 5 — Focus on signal, not noise¶

    Useful signal might include learners successfully applying a method or producing a better-quality output. Noise might include attendance or positive comments alone.

    Step 6 — Use evaluation to improve¶

    Use what you learn to:

    • refine activities
    • identify gaps
    • improve future delivery
    • decide what to keep, change, or remove

    Decision point¶

    How much effort will you invest in meaningful assessment and evaluation?

    • Basic attendance and satisfaction only
    • Some assessment of learning
    • Clear indicators, aligned assessment, and useful evidence

    Your choice affects how well you can understand learning and improve the training.

    Example¶

    • Context: A team has run a climate data training session.
    • Decision: Should they judge success by attendance and participant satisfaction alone?
    • Action: They define indicators linked to the outcomes, such as whether participants can interpret a dataset and explain two practical implications. They assess this through a task, observe how learners perform, and ask a short follow-up question about application after the session.
    • Outcome: They get clearer evidence of learning and a better basis for improving the training.

    Key takeaways¶

    Key takeaway

    Assessment should reflect what learners need to do, not only what they have been told.

    Key takeaway

    Evaluation is most useful when it helps you distinguish meaningful signal from easy but weak indicators.

    Before you move on¶

    You should now have:

    • a small set of clear indicators of success
    • an initial plan for aligned assessment
    • a practical approach to collecting evidence of learning and impact

    Further reading (optional)¶

    • Black, P., & Wiliam, D. (1998) — Assessment and Classroom Learning
      → Supports: formative assessment aligned with learning outcomes
      → Why it matters: shows how assessment for learning improves understanding and performance
      → Source: https://doi.org/10.1080/0969595980050102

    • Patton, M. Q. (2011) — Developmental Evaluation: Applying Complexity Concepts to Enhance Innovation and Use
      → Supports: evaluation focused on real-world impact and learning
      → Why it matters: introduces approaches for evaluating complex interventions like training in dynamic contexts
      → Source: https://doi.org/10.4135/9781442208636

    • Guskey, T. (2000) — Evaluating Professional Development
      → Supports: distinguishing between different levels of training outcomes and impact
      → Why it matters: provides a structured approach to evaluating whether training leads to meaningful change
      → Source: https://doi.org/10.4135/9781452231389
