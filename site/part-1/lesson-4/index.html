    4. Learning Outcomes

    🎯 Learning Outcomes 📍 Guiding Questions
    • Define clear, observable, and actionable learning outcomes
    • Apply backward design to link learning outcomes, evidence, and activities
    • Design activities that enable learners to practise and demonstrate learning outcomes
    • Develop supporting content that directly enables successful participation in activities
    • Diagnose and correct misalignment between learning outcomes, activities, and assessment
    • What should learners be able to do as a result of this training?
    • What will learners need to produce, do, or decide to show they have achieved the intended learning outcomes?
    • Do your planned activities directly support your intended learning outcomes?
    • What content do learners need to successfully participate in the activities?
    • Where might misalignment exist between learning outcomes, activities, assessment, and content?

    Why this matters¶

    In the previous section, you explored how people learn and what supports effective learning.

    This section builds on that by structuring your training design so that learning can lead to clear, observable outcomes.

    Training impact is often not achieved not because of poor delivery, but because:

    • learning outcomes are unclear
    • activities do not support what learners need to do
    • success is not defined meaningfully
    • content does not support the expected learning

    As a result, participants may engage with training but struggle to apply what they learned in practice.

    This section helps you move from...

    “What should I include?”
    to:
    “What should learners be able to do—and how will I support and recognise that?”

    Core concepts¶

    Learning outcomes and alignment

    Effective training starts with clear learning outcomes, defines evidence of success, and then designs activities and assessment that support those outcomes.

    Outputs, outcomes, and impact (from Theory of Change)
    Outputs are immediate products of training, such as worksheets, discussions, or completed tasks. Outcomes are what learners do differently after the training in their real-world context. Impact is the longer-term change that results.

    In this section, we focus specifically on learning outcomes—what participants should be able to do by the end of the training—which contribute to these broader outcomes.

    Backward design
    Building on the theory of change, backward design applies the same logic at the level of training design.

    Instead of starting with content, start with what learners should be able to do, then define how you will recognise that, and then design activities to support it.

    Backward design follows a simple sequence:

    • Learning outcomes → Evidence → Activities

    Evidence helps you judge whether learners have achieved the intended learning outcomes. This can be captured in different ways, including assessment, where learners demonstrate what they can do. You will develop assessment in more detail in later sections.

    Content is developed after activities have been designed, to support those activities.

    It keeps design focused on what matters.

    Constructive alignment
    Alignment means your learning outcomes, activities, and assessment support each other. Learners should practise what they are expected to do, and assessment should reflect that same capability.

    How the design elements fit together¶

    Effective training design involves four key elements. This section focuses on how these elements fit together at a design level. You will develop each of these in more detail in later sections.

    • Learning outcomes — what learners should be able to do by the end
    • Assessment (evidence) — how you will recognise that learning has occurred
    • Activities — how learners will practise and apply skills
    • Content — the information and resources that support learning

    These elements are connected, but they do not have equal roles. Activities are the bridge between learning outcomes and assessment: they are where learning is practised and made visible.

    Design order (how you plan)¶

    When designing training, follow this sequence:

    1. Define learning outcomes
    2. Decide what evidence will demonstrate success
    3. Design activities that enable learners to achieve those outcomes
    4. Develop content that supports those activities

    This helps ensure that everything in your training serves a clear purpose.

    Delivery order (what learners experience)¶

    During the training, learners typically experience:

    • content (input)
    • activities (practice)
    • assessment (demonstration)

    This means the design process works in the opposite direction to the learning experience.

    Key principle¶

    Content should not drive training design.

    Content supports activities, and activities enable learners to work toward the intended learning outcomes.

    Practical guidance¶

    The steps below help you structure your training design. Later sections will support you in developing activities, content, and assessment in more detail.

    Step 1 — Define intended learning outcomes¶

    Strong learning outcomes are:

    • observable
    • action-oriented
    • relevant to real-world use

    For example:

    • “Interpret climate data to identify local risks”
    • “Use a tool to analyse and communicate findings”

    You can use the Bloom’s Taxonomy — Quick Guide to help choose action verbs and vary the level of complexity (e.g. identify vs analyse vs create), but outcomes do not need to progress sequentially through the taxonomy—focus on what learners need to do in practice.

    Step 2 — Define evidence of success¶

    Ask:

    • What would demonstrate that learners have achieved the intended learning outcomes?
    • What would participants produce, do, or decide?

    Keep this manageable. Two or three clear indicators are enough.

    Step 3 — Design aligned activities¶

    For each learning outcome, design activities where learners can:

    • practise the skill
    • apply knowledge
    • receive feedback

    Step 4 — Develop supporting content¶

    Once activities are defined, identify and create only the content needed to support those activities.

    This may include:

    • explanations
    • examples
    • demonstrations
    • resources

    Avoid creating content that is not directly used in an activity.

    Step 5 — Check and refine alignment¶

    Review and revise your design (this will be iterative across later sections):

    • What is taught but not practised?
    • What is practised but not assessed?
    • Which activities do not contribute to learning outcomes?

    Step 6 — Ensure contextual relevance¶

    Make sure learning outcomes reflect:

    • what learners actually need to do
    • the conditions they work within
    • the decisions they are likely to face

    Decision point¶

    How specific and practice-oriented will your learning outcomes be?

    • Broad and general
    • Moderately specific
    • Clear, observable, and linked to practice

    Your choice will affect the quality of your activities, assessment, and evaluation.

    Example¶

    • Context: A team wants to train participants on climate data interpretation.
    • Decision: Should the main learning outcome be “understand climate data” or something more specific?
    • Action: They rewrite the outcome as “interpret a local dataset to identify two practical risks and explain them to a non-specialist audience.” They then define evidence, design a matching activity, and remove unrelated content.
    • Outcome: The training becomes clearer, more practical, and easier to assess.

    Key takeaways¶

    Key takeaway

    Learning outcomes should describe what learners will be able to do, not only what content will be covered.

    Key takeaway

    Alignment is practical: if an activity or assessment does not support a learning outcome, it probably does not belong.

    Before you move on¶

    You should now have:

    • a clearer set of learning outcomes
    • initial indicators or evidence of success
    • a stronger link between outcomes, activities, and assessment
