    2. Finding and Evaluating Existing OER

    Lesson 2 — Finding & Evaluating Open Educational Resources¶

    🎯 Learning Outcomes 📍 Guiding Questions
    • Identify and locate relevant Open Educational Resources (OER) for your training
    • Evaluate OER for alignment, clarity, context fit, adaptability, and licensing
    • Recognise risks and limitations when reusing existing resources
    • Make explicit decisions to reuse, adapt, or create resources
    • Select resources that support your learners, context, and outcomes
    • Where can you find relevant OER?
    • How do you determine whether a resource will work in your training?
    • What makes a resource reusable in practice?
    • What risks are introduced when reusing resources?
    • When should you reuse, adapt, or create a resource?

    Why this matters¶

    In the previous section, you started deciding where you might reuse, adapt, or create materials.

    Now you focus on finding and selecting resources that can support those decisions.

    There are many Open Educational Resources (OER) available. The challenge is not just finding resources — it is finding resources that will work for your learners, your context, and your outcomes.

    Even strong resources may:

    • assume different levels of knowledge
    • rely on tools or access your learners do not have
    • require explanation to make sense
    • not align with what you want learners to achieve

    This does not mean you should avoid using them. It means you need to make practical decisions about how to use them.

    This section helps you move from...

    “What resources can I find?”
    to:
    “Which resources are worth using, adapting, or replacing?”

    Core concepts¶

    Fit for purpose

    A material is useful when it supports your learning outcomes and works for your learners and context.

    A well-designed resource may still not fit your needs. Fit depends on alignment, context, and whether it can realistically be used in your setting.

    Reusability

    Reusable resources are more likely to be understood and used with limited additional explanation.

    A resource may fit your context but still require significant explanation. Improving reusability reduces that effort — for you and for others.

    Contextual assumptions

    All resources are created with assumptions about learners, tools, and environments.

    These assumptions are often linked to what you identified in Part 1 about your learners and context. Making them visible helps you decide whether a resource will work or what needs to change.

    Licensing enables action

    Open licenses define what you are allowed to do with a resource.

    You may identify useful materials, but what you can do with them depends on their license. You will explore this in more detail in the next lesson.

    Practical guidance¶

    Step 1 — Find a small set of candidates¶

    Search for resources using:

    • open repositories and institutional collections
    • platforms like GitHub and Zenodo
    • training resources from organisations and universities
    • targeted web searches filtered for open licenses

    Use specific terms that include:

    • topic
    • learner level
    • type of material

    You do not need to find the “perfect” material — aim for 1–3 promising options to work with.

    Step 2 — Evaluate for fit¶

    Use a small set of lenses to understand whether a material could work in your training:

    Alignment - Does this support your learning outcomes?
    - Is the level appropriate?

    Clarity - Can learners follow this without heavy explanation?
    - Are instructions clear enough to use?

    Context fit - Are examples meaningful for your learners?
    - Does it assume tools or access your learners may not have?

    Adaptability - Can you realistically modify the content and format? - Is it editable and usable?

    Licensing - Are you allowed to reuse or adapt it?
    - Are there restrictions you need to consider?

    You do not need to analyse everything in detail. Focus on what affects how you will use the resource.

    Step 3 — Identify effort and risks¶

    Ask:

    • What might learners struggle with?
    • What would need explanation or adjustment?
    • What assumptions does this resource make?
    • How much effort would it take to make this usable?

    This helps you decide whether the resource is realistic for your context.

    Step 4 — Make a practical decision¶

    How should you use this resource?

    • Reuse → it fits well and needs minimal changes
    • Adapt → it is useful but needs adjustments
    • Create → it does not fit or would take too much effort to adapt

    There is no single correct choice. The goal is to make a decision that is realistic for your context and time.

    Example¶

    • Context: A trainer is designing a workshop on climate data tools for community facilitators.
    • Decision: Should they use an existing online tutorial?
    • Action: They review the tutorial and identify assumptions about internet access, device availability, and prior coding experience. They decide to adapt one part of the tutorial and replace the rest with a simpler, locally relevant activity that they create themselves.
    • Outcome: The training becomes more accessible and easier to deliver, while still drawing on useful elements from existing material.

    In practice¶

    👉 Use Template 12: OER Workflow

    Include:

    • what to do: Identify one resource and evaluate it using alignment, clarity, context fit, adaptability, and licensing
    • focus sections: 1 (Find and Evaluate Resources), 2 (Design Decisions)
    • expected output: A documented decision (reuse, adapt, or create) with clear reasoning
    • approximate time: 20–30 minutes

    👉 Revisit Template 8: Learning Activity Design

    Include:

    • what to do: Check whether the selected resource supports one of your activities with minimal explanation
    • expected output: A refined, adapted, or created activity
    • approximate time: 10–15 minutes

    Suggested process¶

    Step 1 — Select one resource
    Choose a candidate resource relevant to your training.

    Step 2 — Evaluate systematically
    Apply the five criteria (alignment, clarity, context fit, adaptability, licensing).

    Step 3 — Decide
    Choose whether to reuse, adapt, or create.

    Step 4 — Test usability
    Check whether it works within your activity.

    Key takeaways¶

    Key takeaway

    You do not need to find perfect resource — you need to make informed, practical decisions about what to use, adapt, or create.

    Key takeaway

    Evaluating resources helps you understand what will work in your context and what will require change.

    Before you move on¶

    At this point, you should have:

    • at least one resource identified and evaluated
    • an initial decision to reuse, adapt, or create
    • a clearer sense of what fits your learners and context
    • at least one activity that has been reviewed or improved

    These decisions are not final. You will revisit and refine them as you adapt resources and work with licensing in the next lessons.
